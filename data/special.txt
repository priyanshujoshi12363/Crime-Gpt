SPECIAL ACT 1 - UAPA (UNLAWFUL ACTIVITIES PREVENTION ACT, 1967)
Covers terrorism, anti-national activities, secessionist movements, terrorist funding, and banned organisations. Investigating agency is National Investigation Agency (NIA). Bail is nearly impossible - Court must hear Public Prosecutor before granting bail and must be satisfied that accused is not guilty. Maximum punishment is death penalty or life imprisonment. Whoever associates with a terrorist organisation or supports terrorist activities is liable for punishment. Membership of banned organisation is itself an offence. Property acquired through terrorism can be seized and forfeited. Confessions made before police officers of rank Superintendent of Police or above are admissible as evidence. Courts can conduct trials in camera (closed doors). Central Government has power to declare any organisation as unlawful association or terrorist organisation. Appeals can be filed before special tribunals. Offences under this Act are cognizable and non-bailable.

SPECIAL ACT 2 - NDPS (NARCOTIC DRUGS AND PSYCHOTROPIC SUBSTANCES ACT, 1985)
Covers production, manufacture, possession, sale, purchase, transport, import, export, and consumption of narcotic drugs and psychotropic substances. Investigating agency is Narcotics Control Bureau (NCB) or State Police. Bail is extremely difficult for commercial quantities - Court must hear Public Prosecutor and must have reasonable grounds to believe accused is not guilty and will not commit any offence on bail. Punishment based on quantity: Small quantity - rigorous imprisonment up to 1 year. More than small but less than commercial - rigorous imprisonment up to 10 years. Commercial quantity - rigorous imprisonment not less than 10 years which may extend to 20 years and fine not less than Rs. 1 lakh. Repeat offenders for commercial quantity - death penalty possible. Death penalty also possible for financing certain drug-related activities. Consumption of narcotic drugs is punishable with imprisonment up to 6 months. Mandatory testing of seized substances at forensic laboratory within 72 hours. Protection for addict volunteers who voluntarily undergo treatment and cooperate with investigation. Property derived from drug trafficking can be forfeited. Controlled delivery operations permitted for cross-border drug busts. Special courts established for speedy trial of NDPS cases. Presumption of culpable mental state exists against accused. All offences under this Act are cognizable and non-bailable.

SPECIAL ACT 3 - PMLA (PREVENTION OF MONEY LAUNDERING ACT, 2002)
Covers money laundering, concealment, acquisition, possession, and use of proceeds of crime. Investigating agency is Enforcement Directorate (ED). ED can attach property involved in money laundering for up to 180 days which can be extended. ED can also arrest any person believed to be guilty of money laundering. Twin conditions for bail: Court must hear Public Prosecutor AND must be satisfied that accused is not guilty of money laundering and will not commit any offence while on bail. These twin conditions make bail extremely difficult to obtain. All offences under PMLA are cognizable and non-bailable. Maximum punishment is rigorous imprisonment not less than 3 years which may extend to 7 years and fine. For offences linked to NDPS Act, punishment may extend to 10 years. Burden of proof on accused to show that property is not proceeds of crime. Statements made before ED officials during investigation are admissible as evidence. ED can conduct search and seizure without warrant. Adjudicating Authority confirms provisional attachment of property within 180 days. Accused can appeal to Appellate Tribunal within 45 days. Special courts designated for PMLA trials. Investigation and trial of scheduled offences and money laundering offences can be conducted together. Scheduled offences include crimes under IPC, NDPS, PC Act, Arms Act, Wildlife Act, and other serious crimes generating proceeds of crime.

SPECIAL ACT 4 - POCSO (PROTECTION OF CHILDREN FROM SEXUAL OFFENCES ACT, 2012)
Covers all sexual offences against children below 18 years. Child defined as any person below the age of 18 years. Offences include penetrative sexual assault, aggravated penetrative sexual assault, sexual assault, aggravated sexual assault, sexual harassment, and use of child for pornographic purposes. Investigation by local police. Statement of child to be recorded at child's residence or place of choice, preferably by woman police officer not below rank of Sub-Inspector. Statement to be recorded in presence of parents or any person trusted by child. Magistrate to record statement of child victim under section 183 of BNSS. Child not to be detained in police station at night. Medical examination of child to be conducted in presence of parent or person trusted by child. Female child to be examined only by female doctor. Special courts designated for speedy trial. Evidence of child to be recorded within 30 days of taking cognizance. Trial to be completed within 1 year from date of taking cognizance. Presumption of guilt exists in cases of penetrative sexual assault - accused must prove innocence. Burden of proof shifts to accused for several offences. Abetment of or attempt to commit an offence under POCSO is also punishable. Failure to report or record a case is punishable with imprisonment up to 6 months. False complaint or false information is punishable with imprisonment up to 1 year. Identity of child victim cannot be disclosed under any circumstances. Media cannot publish any information that may identify the child. Punishment ranges from 3 years imprisonment to death penalty for aggravated penetrative sexual assault resulting in death or vegetative state of child. All offences under this Act are cognizable and non-bailable.

SPECIAL ACT 5 - IT ACT (INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY ACT, 2000)
Covers cybercrimes including hacking, identity theft, data theft, online fraud, cheating by impersonation, obscene content online, cyber terrorism, and unauthorized access to protected systems. Investigating agency is Cyber Crime Cell under State Police or Central agencies. Section 66 covers hacking with computer system - punishment up to 3 years imprisonment. Section 66B covers dishonestly receiving stolen computer resource - punishment up to 3 years. Section 66C covers identity theft - punishment up to 3 years. Section 66D covers cheating by personation using computer resource - punishment up to 3 years. Section 66E covers violation of privacy - punishment up to 3 years. Section 66F covers cyber terrorism - punishment may extend to imprisonment for life. Section 67 covers publishing or transmitting obscene material in electronic form - punishment up to 3 years on first conviction and 5 years on subsequent conviction. Section 67A covers publishing sexually explicit material - punishment up to 5 years on first conviction and 7 years on subsequent conviction. Section 67B covers depicting children in sexually explicit act - punishment up to 5 years on first conviction and 7 years on subsequent conviction. Section 70 covers protected systems - unauthorized access may lead to imprisonment up to 10 years. Section 72 covers breach of confidentiality - punishment up to 2 years. Section 75 covers offences committed outside India involving computer located in India. Electronic evidence must follow BSA Section 63 certification requirements. CERT-In is national nodal agency for cyber security incident response. Adjudicating Officers can award compensation up to Rs. 5 crore for damages. Cyber Appellate Tribunal hears appeals against Adjudicating Officer orders. All offences under IT Act are cognizable and bailable except cyber terrorism which is non-bailable.

SPECIAL ACT 6 - AFSPA (ARMED FORCES SPECIAL POWERS ACT, 1958)
Grants armed forces special powers in disturbed areas. Currently applies in parts of Assam, Nagaland, Manipur, and Jammu & Kashmir. Armed forces can fire upon any person who is acting in contravention of any law or order if they believe it is necessary to do so for maintenance of public order. Armed forces can arrest any person without warrant who has committed a cognizable offence or against whom a reasonable suspicion exists. Armed forces can enter and search any premises without warrant to make arrest or recovery of arms/ammunition/explosive substances. Armed forces can seize any arms, ammunition, or explosive substances. Any person arrested must be handed over to the nearest police station with least possible delay. Armed forces personnel cannot be prosecuted for any act done in exercise of powers under this Act except with previous sanction of Central Government. Central Government or State Government can declare any area as disturbed area. This Act gives extraordinary powers to armed forces that override normal criminal procedure. No FIR can be registered against armed forces for actions under AFSPA without prior Central Government permission.

SPECIAL ACT 7 - NSA (NATIONAL SECURITY ACT, 1980)
Allows preventive detention of any person for up to 12 months without trial. Central Government or State Government can order detention if satisfied that person is acting in manner prejudicial to defence of India, security of India, security of State, maintenance of public order, or maintenance of supplies and services essential to community. No FIR or chargesheet is required. No trial is conducted. Detention order can be executed anywhere in India. Person detained has right to make representation against detention order. Advisory Board consisting of 3 members who are or have been High Court judges reviews detention within 3 weeks. If Advisory Board reports sufficient cause for detention, Government may confirm detention. If Advisory Board reports insufficient cause, Government shall revoke detention. Maximum period of detention is 12 months from date of detention. Government can revoke or modify detention order at any time. Detention order can be renewed. Grounds of detention must be communicated to detained person within 5 days of detention. Government can withhold facts from disclosure if disclosure is against public interest. This Act is used for preventive detention of potential threats to public order without criminal trial.

SPECIAL ACT 8 - ARMS ACT, 1959
Covers illegal possession, manufacture, sale, transfer, import, export, and use of arms and ammunition. Arms defined to include firearms, swords, and other weapons specified in Schedules. Possession of unlicensed firearm is cognizable and non-bailable offence. Possession of prohibited arms (automatic weapons, rocket launchers, etc.) is punishable with imprisonment not less than 7 years which may extend to life imprisonment and fine. Possession of other firearms without licence is punishable with imprisonment not less than 3 years which may extend to 7 years and fine. Manufacture or sale of arms without licence is punishable with imprisonment not less than 7 years and fine. Import or export of arms without licence is punishable with imprisonment not less than 7 years and fine. Causing death or grievous hurt by illegal arms may lead to death penalty or life imprisonment. Shortening or conversion of firearm without licence is punishable with imprisonment up to 7 years and fine. All offences under this Act are cognizable and most are non-bailable. Mandatory forensic examination of recovered weapons. Licence required for acquisition, possession, and carrying of firearms. District Magistrate is licensing authority. Licence conditions include safe custody, no transfer without permission, and reporting of loss or theft.

SPECIAL ACT 9 - PWDVA (PROTECTION OF WOMEN FROM DOMESTIC VIOLENCE ACT, 2005)
Covers physical abuse, emotional abuse, sexual abuse, verbal abuse, and economic abuse of women within domestic relationship. Domestic relationship includes wife, mother, sister, daughter, or woman living in shared household in relationship like marriage. Respondent can be any adult male or female relative of husband. Complaint can be filed by aggrieved woman or any person on her behalf. Protection Officer appointed in each district to assist women. Court can pass protection order prohibiting respondent from committing domestic violence. Court can pass residence order allowing woman to continue living in shared household. Court can pass monetary relief order for maintenance, medical expenses, and damages. Court can pass custody order for children. Court can pass compensation order for injuries caused by domestic violence. Breach of protection order is cognizable and non-bailable offence punishable with imprisonment up to 1 year or fine up to Rs. 20,000 or both. Magistrate can also frame charges under BNS for cruelty. Proceedings to be completed within 60 days. Woman can also approach civil court for relief. Counselling may be ordered for respondent. Service providers like NGOs can assist women in filing complaints. Notice to Protection Officer must be given within 24 hours of complaint. Woman cannot be evicted from shared household without court order.

SPECIAL ACT 10 - DOWRY PROHIBITION ACT, 1961
Covers giving, taking, abetting giving or taking, and demanding dowry in connection with marriage. Dowry defined as any property or valuable security given or agreed to be given directly or indirectly by one party to marriage to other party or by parents of either party before, at, or after marriage. Giving or taking dowry is punishable with imprisonment not less than 5 years and fine not less than Rs. 15,000 or the amount of value of dowry, whichever is more. Demanding dowry is punishable with imprisonment not less than 6 months which may extend to 2 years and fine which may extend to Rs. 10,000. If dowry death occurs (woman dying within 7 years of marriage from burns or bodily injury under suspicious circumstances and was subjected to dowry harassment before death), the husband or relatives are presumed to have caused dowry death under BNS Section 80. Punishment for dowry death is imprisonment not less than 7 years which may extend to life imprisonment. Agreement for giving or taking dowry is void. Dowry received must be transferred to the woman within 3 months of marriage. Failure to transfer is punishable with imprisonment. Dowry Prohibition Officers appointed to enforce this Act. Offences under this Act are cognizable and non-bailable. All offences are compoundable with court permission.

SPECIAL ACT 11 - SC/ST ACT (SCHEDULED CASTES AND SCHEDULED TRIBES PREVENTION OF ATROCITIES ACT, 1989)
Covers atrocities, discrimination, humiliation, intimidation, and violence against members of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes. Atrocities include forcing to eat or drink obnoxious substance, dumping excreta near house, parading naked, wrongful occupation of land, bonded labour, preventing from voting, false legal proceedings, insult with intent to humiliate in public view, sexual exploitation of women, corrupting or fouling water source, denial of access to public place, and imposing social or economic boycott. Section 18 provides that anticipatory bail does not apply to offences under this Act. Any public servant who wilfully neglects duties under this Act is punishable with imprisonment up to 6 months. Investigation must be conducted by police officer not below rank of Deputy Superintendent of Police. Investigation must be completed within 60 days. Special courts are designated for speedy trial. Trial to be completed within 2 months. Compensation to victims as per scheme notified by State Government. Punishment ranges from 6 months to life imprisonment depending on nature of offence. All offences under this Act are cognizable and non-bailable. Victim and witnesses have right to protection. Court can presume accused committed the offence in certain cases. Appeals to High Court within 180 days. State Government must prepare annual report on implementation.

SPECIAL ACT 12 - PC ACT (PREVENTION OF CORRUPTION ACT, 1988)
Covers bribery, corruption, criminal misconduct, and abuse of position by public servants. Public servant includes government employees, elected representatives, judges, officers of government companies, and persons in service of government. Taking gratification other than legal remuneration is punishable with imprisonment not less than 3 years which may extend to 7 years and fine. Accepting gratification to influence public servant is punishable with imprisonment not less than 3 years which may extend to 7 years and fine. Taking gratification for exercise of personal influence with public servant is punishable with imprisonment not less than 3 years which may extend to 7 years and fine. Criminal misconduct by public servant is punishable with imprisonment not less than 4 years which may extend to 10 years and fine. Habitual bribe-taking is punishable with imprisonment not less than 5 years which may extend to 10 years and fine. Giving bribe to public servant is also an offence - imprisonment up to 7 years or fine or both. Person compelled to give bribe must report to law enforcement within 7 days to avoid prosecution. Investigating agency is Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI) at central level and State Anti-Corruption Bureau (ACB) or Lokayukta at state level. Sanction for prosecution required from competent authority before filing chargesheet. Trial by Special Judge designated under this Act. Trial to be completed within 2 years. Presumption of guilt where gratification accepted. Property disproportionate to known sources of income can be confiscated. Previous sanction not required for public servant who has ceased to hold office.

SPECIAL ACT 13 - JUVENILE JUSTICE (CARE AND PROTECTION OF CHILDREN) ACT, 2015
Covers children in conflict with law (juveniles who committed offences) and children in need of care and protection (abandoned, abused, neglected children). Child defined as person below 18 years. Juvenile Justice Board (JJB) constituted for each district to handle children in conflict with law. JJB comprises Principal Magistrate and two social workers, one of whom must be woman. No child can be sentenced to death or imprisonment for life. No child can be lodged in jail or police lock-up. Observation Homes established for temporary reception of children in conflict with law. Special Homes for rehabilitation of children convicted of offences. Juveniles between 16-18 years who commit heinous offences (punishable with 7+ years) may be tried as adults after preliminary assessment by JJB regarding mental and physical capacity and understanding of consequences. Children's Court conducts trial of such juveniles tried as adults but no death penalty or life imprisonment without possibility of release. Child Welfare Committee (CWC) for each district to handle children in need of care and protection. CWC comprises Chairperson and four members, one of whom must be woman and one expert on children issues. Adoption procedures regulated under this Act. Central Adoption Resource Authority (CARA) is nodal body for adoption. Offences against children (cruelty, employment for begging, giving intoxicating substances, sale, procurement, corporal punishment in institutions) are punishable with imprisonment and fine. All offences against children under this Act are cognizable and non-bailable.

SPECIAL ACT 14 - MCOCA (MAHARASHTRA CONTROL OF ORGANISED CRIME ACT, 1999)
Covers organised crime syndicates operating in Maharashtra. Organised crime defined as continuing unlawful activity by an individual singly or jointly as member of organised crime syndicate. Continuing unlawful activity means at least one charge sheet filed in preceding 10 years. Offences include contract killing, extortion, kidnapping for ransom, robbery, drug trafficking, and other serious crimes committed by organised syndicates. Possessing unaccounted wealth on behalf of organised crime syndicate is also an offence. Bail under MCOCA is nearly impossible - Court must hear Public Prosecutor and must be satisfied that accused is not guilty and will not commit any offence while on bail. Confession made before police officer of rank Superintendent of Police or above is admissible as evidence. Interception of wire, electronic or oral communication permitted with prior permission of competent authority. Special courts designated for MCOCA trials. Trial to be conducted day-to-day after framing of charges. Witness identity can be kept secret. Witness can be examined via video conferencing. Witness protection provisions. Forfeiture of property acquired through organised crime. Punishment ranges from imprisonment for life to death penalty if murder is committed. Conspiracy to commit organised crime is also punishable. Public Prosecutor must be of minimum 7 years practice as advocate. No appeal against interlocutory order. All offences under MCOCA are cognizable and non-bailable.

SPECIAL ACT 15 - ITPA (IMMORAL TRAFFIC PREVENTION ACT, 1956)
Covers prostitution rackets, running brothels, procuring persons for prostitution, detaining persons in premises where prostitution is carried on, and human trafficking for commercial sexual exploitation. Keeping or managing or assisting in keeping or management of brothel is punishable with rigorous imprisonment not less than 2 years which may extend to 3 years and fine for first conviction, and not less than 3 years which may extend to 7 years and fine for subsequent conviction. Living on earnings of prostitution is punishable with imprisonment up to 2 years for first conviction and up to 5 years for subsequent conviction. Procuring, inducing, or taking person for sake of prostitution is punishable with rigorous imprisonment not less than 3 years which may extend to 7 years and fine. If committed against will of person or involving child, punishment is rigorous imprisonment of 7 years to life. Detaining person in premises where prostitution is carried on is punishable with imprisonment not less than 7 years which may extend to 10 years for child victims. Seducing or soliciting for purpose of prostitution is punishable with imprisonment up to 6 months. Trafficking police officers designated to deal with offences under this Act. Protective homes established for rehabilitation of rescued women. State Governments must establish special courts for speedy trial. Victim compensation schemes. All offences under this Act are cognizable.

SPECIAL ACT 16 - CHILD LABOUR (PROHIBITION AND REGULATION) ACT, 1986
Covers employment of children in hazardous occupations and processes. Child defined as person below 14 years. Adolescent defined as person between 14 and 18 years. Complete prohibition on employment of children below 14 years in any occupation or process. Adolescent (14-18 years) cannot be employed in hazardous occupations and processes listed in Schedule. Hazardous occupations include mines, inflammable substances, explosives, railways, ports, automobile workshops, hazardous processes under Factories Act, and chemical mixing. Punishment for employing child is imprisonment not less than 6 months which may extend to 2 years and fine between Rs. 20,000 to Rs. 50,000. Punishment for employing adolescent in hazardous occupation is imprisonment not less than 6 months which may extend to 2 years and fine. Repeat offenders get enhanced punishment - imprisonment not less than 1 year which may extend to 3 years. Parents or guardians permitting child to work also liable. Child Labour Rehabilitation Fund to be established in every district. Employed child entitled to compensation of Rs. 50,000 from employer to be deposited in Child Labour Rehabilitation Fund. Child Labour Technical Advisory Committee advises on additions to hazardous occupations list. Inspectors appointed to enforce this Act. All offences under this Act are cognizable.

SPECIAL ACT 17 - EXPLOSIVES ACT, 1884 AND EXPLOSIVE SUBSTANCES ACT, 1908
Covers illegal manufacture, possession, use, sale, transport, import, and export of explosives and explosive substances. Explosive substance includes any materials for making any explosive substance, any apparatus or machine for causing or facilitating explosion, or any part of such apparatus. Causing explosion likely to endanger life or property is punishable with rigorous imprisonment for life or imprisonment up to 10 years and fine. Attempt to cause explosion is punishable with imprisonment up to 7 years and fine. Making or possessing explosive substance with intent to endanger life or property is punishable with rigorous imprisonment up to 20 years or imprisonment for life and fine. Possession of explosives under suspicious circumstances is an offence under section 5. Punishment for causing explosion resulting in death may extend to death penalty. Directly linked to terror cases, organised crime, and sabotage. Investigating agency is local police, NIA for terror links, or specialised explosive detection units. Forensic examination of explosive substances is mandatory. Licence required for manufacture, possession, use, sale, transport, import, and export of explosives. District Magistrate is licensing authority for most categories. Central Government regulates manufacture of explosives. Petroleum and Explosives Safety Organisation (PESO) is regulatory body. All offences under Explosive Substances Act are cognizable and non-bailable. All offences under Explosives Act are cognizable.

SPECIAL ACT 18 - FOOD SAFETY AND STANDARDS ACT, 2006
Covers adulteration of food, manufacture of sub-standard or misbranded food, unsafe food products, and misleading advertisements about food quality. Food adulteration defined as adding any substance to food that reduces its quality or nature, or makes it injurious to health. Manufacturing, selling, storing, distributing, or importing unsafe food is punishable with imprisonment up to 6 months and fine up to Rs. 1 lakh for general cases. If unsafe food causes non-grievous injury, imprisonment up to 1 year and fine up to Rs. 3 lakhs. If unsafe food causes grievous injury, imprisonment up to 6 years and fine up to Rs. 5 lakhs. If unsafe food causes death, imprisonment not less than 7 years which may extend to life imprisonment and fine not less than Rs. 10 lakhs. Food Safety and Standards Authority of India (FSSAI) is regulatory body. Food Safety Officers appointed in each district with powers to inspect, sample, and seize food products. Food samples must be sent to Food Analyst for testing. Accused can get second sample tested at Referral Food Laboratory. Adjudicating Officers can impose penalties. Food Safety Appellate Tribunal hears appeals. Special courts can be designated for speedy trial of food safety offences. Consumer can also file complaint before Consumer Forum for compensation.

SPECIAL ACT 19 - MOTOR VEHICLES ACT, 1988
Covers criminal offences related to road accidents, rash and negligent driving, drunk driving, driving without licence, and hit and run cases. Causing death by rash or negligent driving (not amounting to culpable homicide) is punishable with imprisonment up to 5 years and fine under Section 304A of BNS. Hit and run causing death attracts imprisonment up to 10 years. Rash driving or riding on public way is punishable with imprisonment up to 1 year or fine up to Rs. 1,000 or both. Driving under influence of alcohol or drugs (more than 30mg per 100ml of blood) is punishable with imprisonment up to 6 months or fine up to Rs. 10,000 or both for first offence, and imprisonment up to 2 years or fine up to Rs. 15,000 or both for subsequent offence. Driving without licence attracts fine up to Rs. 5,000. Driving without insurance attracts fine up to Rs. 2,000. Not wearing helmet attracts fine up to Rs. 1,000 and disqualification of licence for 3 months. Overspeeding, dangerous driving, and jumping red light each attract fines. Motor vehicle causing death or grievous hurt with driver not having valid licence or without insurance leads to enhanced punishment. Motor Accident Claims Tribunal awards compensation to victims under Motor Vehicles Act. Hit and run compensation scheme pays Rs. 2 lakh for death and Rs. 50,000 for grievous injury. Police must register FIR for road accident deaths and injuries. Investigation done by local police or traffic police.
