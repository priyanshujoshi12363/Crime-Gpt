BNS SECTION 1 - SHORT TITLE, COMMENCEMENT AND APPLICATION
CORE CONTENT:
This Act may be called the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023. It extends to the whole of India. It applies to every person guilty of any act or omission contrary to its provisions within India. It also applies to any citizen of India committing an offence outside India, any person on a ship or aircraft registered in India wherever it may be, and any person outside India committing an offence targeting a computer resource located in India.

PROCEDURE: Jurisdiction

The Sanhita applies to all persons within the territory of India, including Indian citizens, foreign nationals, and stateless persons

Indian citizens are liable for offences committed anywhere in the world (extraterritorial jurisdiction)

Any person on an Indian-registered ship or aircraft is subject to this Sanhita regardless of where the ship or aircraft is located

Any person outside India who commits an offence targeting a computer resource located in India is subject to this Sanhita

This applies regardless of the nationality of the person committing the offence

ILLUSTRATION:
X, an Indian citizen, commits murder in Dubai. He can be prosecuted in India under BNS. Y, a foreign national, hacks into a bank server located in India from his home country. He can be prosecuted under BNS. Z, a passenger on an Indian-registered flight flying over international waters, commits theft on the flight. He can be prosecuted under BNS.

BNS SECTION 2 - DEFINITIONS
CORE CONTENT:
Act denotes a series of acts as well as a single act. Animal means any living creature other than a human being. Child means any person below eighteen years of age. Court means a Judge or body of Judges empowered to act judicially. Death means the death of a human being. Dishonestly means doing anything with intention of causing wrongful gain to one person or wrongful loss to another. Document includes electronic and digital records intended to be used as evidence. Fraudulently means doing anything with intention to defraud. Gender pronoun "he" includes male, female, and transgender persons. Government means Central Government or State Government. Injury means any harm illegally caused to any person in body, mind, reputation or property. Judge includes any person empowered to give definitive judgment in legal proceedings. Man means male human being of any age. Movable property includes property of every description except land. Omission denotes a series of omissions as well as a single omission. Person includes any company or association of persons. Public servant includes commissioned officers, judges, court officers, government officers, and persons in service of government or local authorities. Vessel means anything made for conveyance by water. Voluntarily means causing an effect by means intended or known to be likely to cause it. Woman means a female human being of any age. Wrongful gain means gain by unlawful means of property to which the person is not legally entitled. Wrongful loss means loss by unlawful means of property to which the person losing it is legally entitled.

PROCEDURE: Application of Definitions

"Act" includes both a single act and a series of acts forming a course of conduct

"Animal" covers all living creatures other than humans (including birds, reptiles, mammals, etc.)

"Child" means any person below 18 years of age for all purposes under this Sanhita

"Dishonestly" requires proof of intention to cause wrongful gain or wrongful loss

"Document" includes electronic records, digital records, and emails – not just physical documents

"Fraudulently" requires proof of intention to defraud (deceive to cause wrongful gain/loss)

The pronoun "he" throughout the Sanhita includes male, female, and transgender persons

"Injury" covers four categories of harm: physical (body), mental (mind), reputational, and proprietary

"Movable property" includes all property except land and things permanently attached to earth

"Omission" includes both a single omission and a series of omissions

"Person" includes artificial persons like companies, corporations, associations, and firms

"Public servant" includes a wide range of persons in government service, judicial officers, and persons employed by local authorities

"Voluntarily" requires proof that the person intended the effect or knew it was likely to occur

"Wrongful gain" and "wrongful loss" are complementary – gain to one person typically means loss to another

ILLUSTRATION:
X takes Y's mobile phone without Y's consent, intending to keep it. X has acted "dishonestly" because he intended wrongful gain (the phone) to himself and wrongful loss to Y. The phone is "movable property" because it is not land. X is a "person" (individual human). If X is a government employee acting in official capacity, he may be a "public servant" for certain offences.

BNS SECTION 3 - GENERAL EXPLANATIONS
CORE CONTENT:
Every definition of an offence and every penal provision is understood subject to the exceptions contained in Chapter III titled "General Exceptions". When property is in possession of a person's spouse, clerk, or servant on account of that person, it is in that person's possession. Words referring to acts done extend also to illegal omissions. When a criminal act is done by several persons in furtherance of common intention, each is liable as if done by him alone. Where causing of a certain effect partly by act and partly by omission is an offence, it is the same offence. When several persons are engaged in a criminal act, they may be guilty of different offences by means of that act.

PROCEDURE: Interpretation Rules

All penal provisions must be read together with the General Exceptions (sections 14-44)

Even if an act falls within the definition of an offence, it may not be punishable if it falls under a General Exception

Property in possession of a spouse, clerk, or servant on account of the person is deemed to be in that person's possession for legal purposes

References to "acts" include illegal omissions (failures to act when there is a legal duty to act)

Common Intention (Section 3(5)): When two or more persons do a criminal act in furtherance of common intention, each is liable as if he alone did the act

When an offence can be committed partly by act and partly by omission, it is the same offence regardless of the mix

Different persons engaged in the same criminal act may be guilty of different offences depending on their individual intentions and knowledge

ILLUSTRATION:
A and B both attack C. A intends to kill C, B only intends to hurt C. C dies. A is guilty of murder, B is guilty of culpable homicide or hurt depending on the circumstances, even though they were engaged in the same criminal act. A's clerk has stolen goods in his possession on A's behalf – A is deemed to be in possession of those goods.

BNS SECTION 4 - PUNISHMENTS
CORE CONTENT:
The punishments to which offenders are liable under this Sanhita are: (a) Death, (b) Imprisonment for life, (c) Imprisonment of two descriptions - rigorous (with hard labour) or simple, (d) Forfeiture of property, (e) Fine, and (f) Community Service.

PROCEDURE: Types of Punishment

Death: Sentence that the offender shall be hanged by the neck till dead. Only for the most serious offences (murder, certain terrorist acts, certain rape offences)

Imprisonment for life: Imprisonment for the remainder of the person's natural life, reckoned as equivalent to 20 years for calculating fractions

Rigorous imprisonment: Imprisonment with hard labour (the convict is required to perform physical work)

Simple imprisonment: Imprisonment without hard labour (only confinement)

Forfeiture of property: Government takes ownership of the convict's property

Fine: Monetary penalty – amount may be unlimited unless specified otherwise, but shall not be excessive

Community service: Work ordered by Court as punishment that benefits the community, for which the convict shall not be entitled to any remuneration

KEYWORDS:

punishments

forfeiture of property

fine

community service

sentencing

ILLUSTRATION:
X is convicted of murder. The Court may sentence him to death or imprisonment for life. Y is convicted of theft. The Court may sentence him to rigorous imprisonment for 2 years (with hard labour) or simple imprisonment (without hard labour) or fine or community service. Z is convicted of defamation – the Court may sentence him to community service such as cleaning a public park.

BNS SECTION 5 - COMMUTATION OF SENTENCE
CORE CONTENT:
The appropriate Government may, without the consent of the offender, commute any punishment under this Sanhita to any other punishment in accordance with section 474 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023. The appropriate Government means the Central Government where sentence is death or offence relates to Union executive power, and the State Government where sentence relates to State executive power.

PROCEDURE: Commutation of Sentence

The appropriate Government can reduce or change a punishment without the offender's consent

Death sentence may be commuted to imprisonment for life

Imprisonment for life may be commuted to imprisonment for a term not less than seven years

Imprisonment for seven years or more may be commuted to imprisonment for a term not less than three years

Imprisonment for less than seven years may be commuted to fine

Rigorous imprisonment may be commuted to simple imprisonment for any term

Central Government handles commutation for death sentences and offences relating to Union executive power

State Government handles commutation for all other sentences

KEYWORDS:

commutation

appropriate government

Central Government

State Government

reduction of sentence

without consent

ILLUSTRATION:
X is sentenced to death by the Court. The Central Government may commute this sentence to imprisonment for life without asking X's permission. Y is sentenced to rigorous imprisonment for 10 years by the Court. The State Government may commute this to simple imprisonment for 7 years.

BNS SECTION 6 - FRACTIONS OF TERMS OF PUNISHMENT
CORE CONTENT:
In calculating fractions of terms of punishment, imprisonment for life shall be reckoned as equivalent to imprisonment for twenty years unless otherwise provided.

PROCEDURE: Calculating Fractions

When a law refers to a fraction of imprisonment for life, treat imprisonment for life as 20 years

Example: "one-half of imprisonment for life" means 10 years

Example: "one-fourth of imprisonment for life" means 5 years

This is only for calculation purposes – actual imprisonment for life may be longer than 20 years

Unless another provision explicitly states otherwise

KEYWORDS:

fractions

calculation

imprisonment for life

twenty years

sentencing calculation

ILLUSTRATION:
Section 56 states that abetment of an offence punishable with imprisonment for life, if the offence is not committed, is punishable with imprisonment up to one-fourth of the longest term provided for the offence. One-fourth of imprisonment for life = one-fourth of 20 years = 5 years.

BNS SECTION 7 - SENTENCE MAY BE WHOLLY OR PARTLY RIGOROUS OR SIMPLE
CORE CONTENT:
In every case where an offender is punishable with imprisonment of either description, the Court may direct that imprisonment shall be wholly rigorous, wholly simple, or partly rigorous and partly simple.

PROCEDURE: Mixed Sentencing

Court has discretion to choose the nature of imprisonment when the law provides for "imprisonment of either description"

Court may order:

(i) Wholly rigorous imprisonment

(ii) Wholly simple imprisonment

(iii) Partly rigorous and partly simple imprisonment (split the sentence)

Rigorous imprisonment requires hard labour (e.g., physical work in jail)

Simple imprisonment only requires confinement (no hard labour)

KEYWORDS:

rigorous imprisonment

simple imprisonment

mixed sentence

court discretion

hard labour

ILLUSTRATION:
X is convicted of theft punishable with imprisonment of either description for 2 years. The Court may order: (a) 2 years rigorous imprisonment, OR (b) 2 years simple imprisonment, OR (c) 1 year rigorous + 1 year simple.

BNS SECTION 8 - AMOUNT OF FINE, LIABILITY IN DEFAULT OF PAYMENT
CORE CONTENT:
Where no sum is expressed to which a fine may extend, the amount of fine is unlimited but shall not be excessive. The Court may direct that in default of payment of fine, the offender shall suffer imprisonment for a certain term not exceeding one-fourth of the maximum term fixed for the offence. Imprisonment in default of payment of fine shall terminate when fine is paid or levied.

PROCEDURE: Fine and Default

If the law does not specify a maximum fine, the Court can impose any amount but it must not be excessive

Court must consider the offender's ability to pay when fixing fine amount

Default imprisonment cannot exceed one-fourth of the maximum imprisonment term for the offence

Example: Offence has maximum imprisonment of 4 years – default imprisonment maximum is 1 year

Imprisonment in default is not a substitute for the substantive sentence – it is additional

The default imprisonment ends as soon as the fine is paid or recovered by the Government

The Court may order payment in installments

KEYWORDS:

fine

default imprisonment

excessive fine

ability to pay

installments

recovery of fine

ILLUSTRATION:
X is convicted of an offence punishable with imprisonment up to 4 years. The Court sentences X to 2 years imprisonment and a fine of ₹50,000. In default of payment of fine, the Court orders an additional 6 months imprisonment (one-fourth of 4 years = 1 year, so 6 months is within limit). If X pays the fine after 2 months of default imprisonment, he is released immediately.

BNS SECTION 9 - LIMIT OF PUNISHMENT OF OFFENCE MADE UP OF SEVERAL OFFENCES
CORE CONTENT:
Where anything which is an offence is made up of parts, any of which parts is itself an offence, the offender shall not be punished with punishment of more than one of such offences. Where several acts constitute different offences when combined, the offender shall not be punished with more severe punishment than the Court could award for any one of such offences.

PROCEDURE: Limitation on Punishment for Composite Offences

When an offence consists of several parts, each part being a separate offence, the offender cannot be punished for more than one of those parts

Example: Assault causing hurt – assault is an offence, causing hurt is an offence. The offender cannot be punished separately for both

When several acts together constitute a different offence from the individual acts, the punishment cannot exceed what the Court could impose for any one of the individual acts

This prevents double punishment for the same conduct

KEYWORDS:

composite offence

multiple offences

limit of punishment

double punishment

constituent parts

ILLUSTRATION:
X commits assault on Y, and in the same act causes hurt. Assault is punishable with 3 months, hurt with 1 year. The offence of "assault causing hurt" is made up of both parts. X cannot be punished separately for assault and for hurt – he can only be punished for one of them, or for the composite offence.

BNS SECTION 10 - PUNISHMENT WHEN DOUBTFUL OF WHICH OFFENCE
CORE CONTENT:
In cases where judgment is given that a person is guilty of one of several offences but it is doubtful of which, the offender shall be punished for the offence for which the lowest punishment is provided if the same punishment is not provided for all.

PROCEDURE: Sentencing in Case of Doubt

Applies when the Court finds the accused guilty but cannot determine which specific offence among several he committed

The Court must apply the punishment for the offence with the lowest prescribed punishment

If all offences have the same punishment, the Court can impose that punishment

This benefits the accused – the doubt is resolved in his favour

KEYWORDS:

doubtful offence

lowest punishment

benefit of doubt

sentencing

ILLUSTRATION:
The Court finds X guilty of either theft (punishable with 3 years) or criminal misappropriation (punishable with 2 years) but cannot determine which. X shall be punished for criminal misappropriation (2 years) because it has the lower punishment.

BNS SECTION 11 - SOLITARY CONFINEMENT
CORE CONTENT:
The Court may order solitary confinement for a portion of imprisonment not exceeding three months in the whole according to scale: not exceeding one month if imprisonment does not exceed six months; not exceeding two months if imprisonment exceeds six months but not one year; not exceeding three months if imprisonment exceeds one year.

PROCEDURE: Solitary Confinement Limits

Total Imprisonment	Maximum Solitary Confinement
Up to 6 months	1 month
More than 6 months but less than 1 year	2 months
1 year or more	3 months
Solitary confinement means the prisoner is kept separate from other prisoners without any contact

The Court may order solitary confinement for a portion of the total sentence

The limits in this section are the maximum allowed

Solitary confinement must comply with additional limits in section 12

KEYWORDS:

solitary confinement

segregation

imprisonment

sentencing limits

ILLUSTRATION:
X is sentenced to 8 months imprisonment. The Court may order up to 2 months of solitary confinement as part of that sentence. If the sentence is 2 years, the Court may order up to 3 months of solitary confinement.

BNS SECTION 12 - LIMIT OF SOLITARY CONFINEMENT
CORE CONTENT:
Solitary confinement shall not exceed fourteen days at a time with intervals of not less duration than such periods. When imprisonment exceeds three months, solitary confinement shall not exceed seven days in any one month of imprisonment awarded.

PROCEDURE: Periodic Limits on Solitary Confinement

No single period of solitary confinement can exceed 14 days

Between two periods of solitary confinement, there must be an interval at least as long as the preceding solitary period

Example: 10 days solitary → at least 10 days normal imprisonment before next solitary period

When total imprisonment exceeds 3 months, solitary confinement cannot exceed 7 days in any single month of the total sentence

These limits apply even if the total solitary confinement under section 11 is higher

KEYWORDS:

solitary confinement

periodic limits

fourteen days

seven days

intervals

ILLUSTRATION:
X is sentenced to 2 years imprisonment with 3 months solitary confinement. In any given month of the 2-year sentence, X cannot be kept in solitary confinement for more than 7 days. No single period of solitary confinement can exceed 14 days.

BNS SECTION 13 - ENHANCED PUNISHMENT FOR CERTAIN OFFENCES AFTER PREVIOUS CONVICTION
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever having been convicted by a Court in India of an offence punishable under Chapter X or Chapter XVII with imprisonment of three years or upwards, and is subsequently guilty of any offence punishable under either Chapter with like imprisonment, shall be subject to imprisonment for life or imprisonment up to ten years.

PROCEDURE: Enhanced Punishment for Repeat Offenders

First condition: Previous conviction in India for an offence under Chapter X (Offences against property) or Chapter XVII (Offences against property – specific) punishable with imprisonment of 3 years or more

Second condition: Subsequent conviction for any offence under the same Chapters punishable with imprisonment of 3 years or more

If both conditions satisfied, the Court may sentence the offender to:

(i) Imprisonment for life, OR

(ii) Imprisonment up to ten years (instead of the normal maximum)

This section does not apply if the previous conviction was for an offence with less than 3 years imprisonment

KEYWORDS:

enhanced punishment

previous conviction

repeat offender

habitual offender

Chapter X

Chapter XVII

imprisonment for life

ILLUSTRATION:
X is convicted of theft (Chapter XVII) and sentenced to 4 years imprisonment. Five years later, X is again convicted of robbery (Chapter XVII). Normally, robbery is punishable with up to 10 years. Under this section, X may be sentenced to imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life) or up to 10 years.

BNS SECTION 14 - ACT DONE BY PERSON BOUND BY LAW
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing is an offence which is done by a person who is, or who by reason of a mistake of fact and not by reason of mistake of law in good faith believes himself to be, bound by law to do it.

PROCEDURE: General Exception – Legal Obligation

This is a General Exception – acts falling under this section are not offences

Two scenarios:

(i) Person is actually bound by law to do the act

(ii) Person in good faith believes he is bound by law to do the act (due to mistake of FACT, not mistake of LAW)

Mistake of law is NOT a defence – ignorance of law is no excuse

Mistake of fact can be a defence if the person acted in good faith

Good faith means acting with due care and attention

KEYWORDS:

general exception

bound by law

mistake of fact

mistake of law

good faith

defence

ILLUSTRATION:
A police officer arrests X under a valid warrant. The arrest is not an offence because the officer is bound by law to execute the warrant. A police officer arrests Y believing in good faith (due to incorrect information from a reliable source) that a warrant has been issued, but no warrant actually exists. The arrest is not an offence because of mistake of fact. A person who does not know that theft is illegal cannot claim mistake of law as a defence.

BNS SECTION 15 - ACT OF JUDGE WHEN ACTING JUDICIALLY
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing is an offence which is done by a Judge when acting judicially in the exercise of any power which is, or which in good faith he believes to be, given to him by law.

PROCEDURE: Judicial Immunity

This section protects Judges from criminal prosecution for their judicial acts

The act must be done while acting judicially (not administrative or personal capacity)

The Judge must be exercising a power that is actually given by law, or which he in good faith believes is given by law

Good faith belief extends protection even if the Judge is mistaken about the scope of his power

This immunity is essential for judicial independence

KEYWORDS:

judge

judicial act

judicial immunity

good faith

protection

ILLUSTRATION:
Judge A sentences X to imprisonment. Even if the sentence is later found to be illegal, Judge A cannot be prosecuted for an offence because he was acting judicially. Judge B issues a warrant believing in good faith that he has the power to do so, but he actually does not. The act is still not an offence.

BNS SECTION 16 - ACT DONE PURSUANT TO JUDGMENT OR ORDER OF COURT
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing which is done in pursuance of or warranted by the judgment or order of a Court, if done whilst such judgment or order remains in force, is an offence, provided the person doing the act in good faith believes that the Court had jurisdiction.

PROCEDURE: Protection for Acts Under Court Orders

Acts done under a valid Court order are not offences

The Court order must be in force at the time the act is done

The person executing the order must act in good faith and believe the Court had jurisdiction

This protects court officers, police, and others who execute court orders

If the Court order is later set aside, acts done while it was in force remain protected

KEYWORDS:

court order

judgment

execution of order

good faith

jurisdiction

ILLUSTRATION:
A Court orders attachment of X's property. The bailiff seizes the property under the order. Even if the order is later set aside on appeal, the bailiff cannot be prosecuted for trespass or theft because he acted under a valid court order in good faith.

BNS SECTION 17 - ACT DONE BY PERSON JUSTIFIED BY LAW
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing is an offence which is done by any person who is justified by law, or who by reason of a mistake of fact and not by reason of mistake of law in good faith believes himself to be justified by law, in doing it.

PROCEDURE: Legal Justification Defence

This is a General Exception for acts justified by law

Two scenarios:

(i) Person is actually justified by law to do the act

(ii) Person in good faith believes he is justified by law (due to mistake of FACT, not mistake of LAW)

Mistake of fact defence available if the person acted in good faith

Mistake of law is never a defence

Similar to section 14 but applies to "justified" acts rather than "bound" acts

KEYWORDS:

justified by law

mistake of fact

mistake of law

good faith

defence

ILLUSTRATION:
A security guard uses reasonable force to remove a trespasser. The law justifies this act. A citizen who in good faith believes he is justified to arrest a person he saw committing a crime (citizen's arrest) is protected even if his belief was based on mistaken facts.

BNS SECTION 18 - ACCIDENT IN DOING A LAWFUL ACT
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing is an offence which is done by accident or misfortune, and without any criminal intention or knowledge, in the doing of a lawful act in a lawful manner by lawful means and with proper care and caution.

PROCEDURE: Accident Defence

This is a General Exception for accidental harm

All five conditions must be satisfied:

(i) The act was done by accident or misfortune (unintended, unforeseen)

(ii) Without any criminal intention or knowledge

(iii) The act being done was lawful

(iv) Done in a lawful manner by lawful means

(v) Done with proper care and caution

If any of these conditions is missing, the defence fails

"Proper care and caution" means what a reasonable person would do in the circumstances

KEYWORDS:

accident

misfortune

unintended harm

proper care

lawful act

criminal intention

ILLUSTRATION:
X is driving his car within the speed limit, following all traffic rules, with full attention. A child suddenly runs onto the road from behind a parked car. X cannot stop in time and hits the child. X has not committed an offence because it was an accident without criminal intention, done while performing a lawful act with proper care.

BNS SECTION 19 - ACT LIKELY TO CAUSE HARM BUT DONE WITHOUT CRIMINAL INTENT
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing is an offence merely by reason of its being done with knowledge that it is likely to cause harm, if done without any criminal intention to cause harm, and in good faith for the purpose of preventing or avoiding other harm to person or property.

PROCEDURE: Necessity Defence

This is a General Exception for acts done to prevent greater harm

Three conditions:

(i) The person knows the act is likely to cause some harm

(ii) No criminal intention to cause that harm

(iii) Act done in good faith to prevent or avoid other (greater) harm to person or property

This is the "lesser evil" or "necessity" defence

The harm prevented must be greater than the harm caused

Good faith requires due care and attention

KEYWORDS:

necessity defence

lesser evil

good faith

preventing harm

without criminal intention

ILLUSTRATION:
X sees a child on the railway tracks with a train approaching. X pushes the child off the tracks, knowing the push may cause minor injury to the child. X has not committed an offence because he acted in good faith to prevent greater harm (death) and had no intention to cause the minor injury.

BNS SECTION 20 - ACT OF A CHILD UNDER SEVEN YEARS OF AGE
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing is an offence which is done by a child under seven years of age.

PROCEDURE: Child Exception – Absolute Immunity

This is a General Exception providing absolute immunity to children under 7

No criminal liability attaches regardless of the act committed

No requirement to prove incapacity – it is presumed by law

Children under 7 are conclusively presumed to be incapable of forming criminal intent

This is based on the principle of doli incapax (incapable of crime)

KEYWORDS:

child

under seven years

absolute immunity

doli incapax

criminal capacity

ILLUSTRATION:
X is 6 years old. He takes Y's mobile phone from Y's bag. X has not committed theft because he is under 7 years of age. No criminal proceedings can be initiated against X.

BNS SECTION 21 - ACT OF A CHILD ABOVE SEVEN AND UNDER TWELVE OF IMMATURE UNDERSTANDING
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing is an offence which is done by a child above seven and under twelve years of age who has not attained sufficient maturity of understanding to judge of the nature and consequences of his conduct on that occasion.

PROCEDURE: Child Exception – Conditional Immunity for Ages 7-12

This section applies to children between 7 and 12 years of age

Immunity is not automatic – the Court must determine if the child had sufficient maturity of understanding

If the child has not attained sufficient maturity to judge nature and consequences of his conduct, the act is not an offence

The burden is on the prosecution to prove that the child had sufficient maturity

Maturity depends on the child's intellectual development, upbringing, understanding of right and wrong, and the specific circumstances of the act

KEYWORDS:

child

seven to twelve years

maturity of understanding

conditional immunity

doli incapax

ILLUSTRATION:
X is 10 years old. He takes Y's mobile phone knowing that it is wrong and that he could be punished. A Court finds he had sufficient maturity. He can be prosecuted. Z is 9 years old with intellectual disability. He takes a chocolate from a shop not understanding that it is wrong. The Court finds he lacked sufficient maturity – no offence.

BNS SECTION 22 - ACT OF A PERSON OF UNSOUND MIND
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing is an offence which is done by a person who, at the time of doing it, by reason of unsoundness of mind, is incapable of knowing the nature of the act, or that he is doing what is either wrong or contrary to law.

PROCEDURE: Insanity Defence (McNaughten Rules)

This is the legal test for insanity defence (based on McNaughten Rules)

The accused must prove (on balance of probabilities) that he was of unsound mind at the time of the act

Three possible ways the accused lacks capacity:

(i) Incapable of knowing the nature of the act (does not understand what he is doing)

(ii) Incapable of knowing that what he is doing is wrong (morally wrong)

(iii) Incapable of knowing that what he is doing is contrary to law (legally wrong)

The unsoundness of mind must exist at the time of the act, not before or after

Partial or intermittent insanity does not provide a complete defence if the person knew what he was doing at the time

If successful, the Court will record a finding of not guilty by reason of insanity and order detention under BNSS section 374

KEYWORDS:

unsound mind

insanity defence

McNaughten Rules

nature of the act

wrong

contrary to law

mental illness

ILLUSTRATION:
X, suffering from schizophrenia, believes he is killing a demon when he actually kills Y. X is incapable of knowing the nature of his act (he thinks he is killing a demon, not a human) – not an offence. X, in a fit of rage due to depression, kills Y knowing it is wrong – not covered because he knew what he was doing was wrong.

BNS SECTION 23 - ACT OF PERSON INCAPABLE BY REASON OF INTOXICATION CAUSED AGAINST HIS WILL
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing is an offence which is done by a person who, at the time of doing it, by reason of intoxication, is incapable of knowing the nature of the act or that he is doing what is wrong, provided the intoxicant was administered without his knowledge or against his will.

PROCEDURE: Involuntary Intoxication Defence

This defence applies only when intoxication is involuntary (against will or without knowledge)

Voluntary intoxication (drinking willingly) is NOT a defence

The intoxication must be so severe that the person is incapable of knowing:

(i) The nature of the act, OR

(ii) That what he is doing is wrong

The intoxicant must have been administered:

(i) Without his knowledge (e.g., drink spiked without his awareness), OR

(ii) Against his will (e.g., forced to drink at gunpoint)

Burden of proof is on the accused to show the intoxication was involuntary

KEYWORDS:

intoxication

involuntary intoxication

against will

without knowledge

incapacity

defence

ILLUSTRATION:
X's drink is spiked without his knowledge. He becomes severely intoxicated and, not knowing what he is doing, assaults Y. X has a defence. Z willingly drinks alcohol, becomes intoxicated, and assaults Y. Z has no defence.

BNS SECTION 24 - OFFENCE REQUIRING PARTICULAR INTENT OR KNOWLEDGE COMMITTED BY INTOXICATED PERSON
CORE CONTENT:
A person who does an act in a state of intoxication shall be liable as if he had the same knowledge as he would have had if not intoxicated, unless the intoxicant was administered without his knowledge or against his will.

PROCEDURE: Constructive Knowledge for Intoxicated Persons

For offences that require particular intent or knowledge, an intoxicated person is treated as having the same knowledge as a sober person

The law imputes knowledge to the intoxicated person

Exception: Involuntary intoxication (as in section 23) – the person is not liable

This prevents people from using voluntary intoxication as a defence to reduce their liability

KEYWORDS:

intoxication

constructive knowledge

imputed knowledge

voluntary intoxication

intent

ILLUSTRATION:
X voluntarily drinks alcohol and becomes highly intoxicated. He shoots a gun into a crowd, killing Y. Even though X was too intoxicated to know what he was doing, he is treated as having the knowledge that firing a gun into a crowd is likely to cause death. He is liable for murder or culpable homicide.

BNS SECTION 25 - ACT NOT INTENDED TO CAUSE DEATH OR GRIEVOUS HURT, DONE BY CONSENT
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing which is not intended to cause death or grievous hurt, and which is not known to be likely to cause death or grievous hurt, is an offence by reason of any harm caused to any person above eighteen years of age who has given consent, express or implied, to suffer that harm.

PROCEDURE: Consent Defence for Harm

This defence applies only when:

(i) The person harmed is above 18 years of age

(ii) The person gave consent (express or implied) to suffer the harm

(iii) The act was not intended to cause death or grievous hurt

(iv) The act was not known to be likely to cause death or grievous hurt

Consent can be express (verbal or written) or implied (from conduct)

This covers lawful sporting activities (boxing, football, etc.) and other consensual acts

Does NOT apply if death or grievous hurt is intended or likely

KEYWORDS:

consent

express consent

implied consent

sporting injuries

harm

above eighteen years

ILLUSTRATION:
Two boxers, A and B, both above 18, consent to a boxing match. A punches B in the face, causing hurt. A has not committed an offence because the harm was consented to and death/grievous hurt was not intended. If A hits B with a metal pipe, he would be liable because such harm was not contemplated by the consent.

BNS SECTION 26 - ACT NOT INTENDED TO CAUSE DEATH, DONE BY CONSENT IN GOOD FAITH FOR PERSON'S BENEFIT
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing which is not intended to cause death is an offence by reason of any harm which it may cause to any person for whose benefit it is done in good faith, and who has given consent to suffer that harm or take the risk of that harm.

PROCEDURE: Medical Consent Defence

This defence applies to medical treatment and similar situations

Conditions:

(i) The act is done for the benefit of the person harmed

(ii) Done in good faith (with due care and attention)

(iii) The person consented to suffer the harm or take the risk

(iv) The act was not intended to cause death

Even if harm results, the medical professional is not criminally liable

Consent must be informed consent (patient understands the risks)

This does not cover negligence – good faith requires due care

KEYWORDS:

medical consent

informed consent

good faith

benefit of patient

medical treatment

surgical risk

ILLUSTRATION:
A surgeon performs an operation with the patient's consent. Despite all due care, complications arise and the patient suffers harm. The surgeon has not committed an offence because the act was done in good faith for the patient's benefit with consent. If the surgeon operates drunk and the patient dies, the defence does not apply because there was no good faith.

BNS SECTION 27 - ACT DONE IN GOOD FAITH FOR BENEFIT OF CHILD OR PERSON OF UNSOUND MIND
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing done in good faith for the benefit of a person under twelve years of age or person of unsound mind, by or by consent of the guardian, is an offence by reason of any harm caused, except intentional causing of death or attempting to cause death, causing death for purposes other than preventing death or curing grievous disease, or voluntarily causing grievous hurt.

PROCEDURE: Guardian Consent Defence

Applies to children under 12 and persons of unsound mind

The act must be done for their benefit

Done by the guardian or with the guardian's consent

Done in good faith

Even if harm is caused, no offence – EXCEPT for:

(i) Intentionally causing death

(ii) Attempting to cause death

(iii) Causing death for purposes other than preventing death or curing grievous disease

(iv) Voluntarily causing grievous hurt

For these excepted acts, the defence is not available

KEYWORDS:

guardian

child

unsound mind

good faith

benefit

consent

ILLUSTRATION:
A guardian consents to a medical procedure for a 10-year-old child that is necessary for the child's health. The procedure causes pain and minor harm. No offence. If the guardian authorizes a procedure intended to kill the child, the defence does not apply.

BNS SECTION 28 - CONSENT KNOWN TO BE GIVEN UNDER FEAR OR MISCONCEPTION
CORE CONTENT:
Consent is not valid if given by a person under fear of injury or under misconception of fact and the person doing the act knows of such fear or misconception, or if given by a person who from unsoundness of mind or intoxication is unable to understand the nature and consequence of that to which he gives consent, or if given by a person under twelve years of age.

PROCEDURE: Invalid Consent

Consent is NOT valid in the following situations:

(i) Given under fear of injury AND the person doing the act knows of that fear

(ii) Given under misconception of fact AND the person doing the act knows of that misconception

(iii) Given by a person of unsound mind who cannot understand the nature and consequences

(iv) Given by an intoxicated person who cannot understand the nature and consequences

(v) Given by a person under 12 years of age

Consent obtained through fraud or misrepresentation is not valid consent

Consent by a child under 12 is automatically invalid

KEYWORDS:

consent

invalid consent

fear

misconception

fraud

unsound mind

intoxication

child

ILLUSTRATION:
X threatens to kill Y unless Y consents to give his property. Y gives "consent". X knows of the fear. The consent is invalid. X tells Y that he will give Y a job if Y gives him ₹10,000. X has no job to give. Y consents based on this false promise. The consent is invalid because it is obtained by misconception.

BNS SECTION 29 - EXCLUSION OF ACTS WHICH ARE OFFENCES INDEPENDENTLY OF HARM CAUSED
CORE CONTENT:
The exceptions in sections 25, 26 and 27 do not extend to acts which are offences independently of any harm they may cause to the person giving consent. Causing miscarriage is an offence independently of any harm to the woman, and consent does not justify the act.

PROCEDURE: Non-Compoundable Offences Excluded from Consent Defence

Sections 25, 26, and 27 provide defences based on consent

This section clarifies that those defences do NOT apply to acts that are offences even without causing harm

Example: Causing miscarriage is an offence regardless of harm – consent of the woman does not justify it

Other offences that are punishable regardless of harm also cannot be justified by consent

KEYWORDS:

consent defence

exclusion

miscarriage

independent offence

non-compoundable

ILLUSTRATION:
A woman consents to an abortion performed by a non-medical professional. Causing miscarriage is an offence under section 88 regardless of whether harm is caused. The consent does not make the act lawful. X cannot rely on section 25, 26, or 27 to claim that the woman's consent justifies the miscarriage.

BNS SECTION 30 - ACT DONE IN GOOD FAITH FOR BENEFIT OF PERSON WITHOUT CONSENT
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing is an offence by reason of any harm caused to a person for whose benefit it is done in good faith, without that person's consent, if it is impossible to signify consent or the person is incapable of giving consent and has no guardian, except intentional causing of death, causing death for purposes other than preventing death or curing grievous disease, or voluntary causing of hurt for purposes other than preventing death or hurt.

PROCEDURE: Emergency Medical Treatment Without Consent

Applies when consent cannot be obtained because:

(i) It is impossible to signify consent (e.g., person unconscious), OR

(ii) The person is incapable of giving consent (e.g., child, unsound mind) and has no guardian

The act must be done in good faith for the person's benefit

Defence applies even though no consent was obtained

EXCEPTIONS (defence does NOT apply):

(i) Intentionally causing death

(ii) Causing death for purposes other than preventing death or curing grievous disease

(iii) Voluntarily causing hurt for purposes other than preventing death or hurt

KEYWORDS:

emergency treatment

without consent

good faith

medical emergency

incapable of consent

no guardian

ILLUSTRATION:
An unconscious accident victim is brought to the hospital. The doctors perform life-saving surgery without consent. No offence. If a doctor performs an unnecessary amputation on an unconscious patient solely for research purposes (not for the patient's benefit), the defence does not apply.

BNS SECTION 31 - COMMUNICATION MADE IN GOOD FAITH
CORE CONTENT:
No communication made in good faith is an offence by reason of any harm to the person to whom it is made, if it is made for the benefit of that person.

PROCEDURE: Good Faith Communication Defence

This protects truthful communications made for someone's benefit

Conditions:

(i) Communication made in good faith (with due care and honest intention)

(ii) Made for the benefit of the person receiving the communication

Even if the communication causes harm (e.g., emotional distress), it is not an offence

Covers situations like doctors giving bad news, employers warning employees about dangerous conditions, etc.

Does not protect false statements or statements made with malicious intent

KEYWORDS:

communication

good faith

benefit of recipient

truthful communication

defamation

ILLUSTRATION:
A doctor honestly tells a patient that he has terminal cancer. The patient suffers emotional distress. The doctor has not committed any offence because the communication was made in good faith for the patient's benefit. X falsely spreads rumours about Y causing Y harm – this is not protected.

BNS SECTION 32 - ACT TO WHICH A PERSON IS COMPELLED BY THREATS
CORE CONTENT:
Except murder and offences against the State punishable with death, nothing is an offence which is done by a person compelled to do it by threats which reasonably cause apprehension that instant death will otherwise be the consequence.

PROCEDURE: Duress Defence (Compulsion by Threats)

This defence applies when a person is forced to commit an act under threat of instant death

Conditions:

(i) The threat reasonably causes apprehension of instant death

(ii) The person acts under compulsion

The defence does NOT apply to:

(i) Murder

(ii) Offences against the State punishable with death (e.g., waging war against India)

The threat must be of death – threat of grievous hurt is not sufficient

The death threatened must be "instant" – immediate, not future

The apprehension must be reasonable – what a reasonable person would feel

KEYWORDS:

duress

compulsion

threats

instant death

reasonable apprehension

defence

murder exception

ILLUSTRATION:
X is held at gunpoint and told "Drive this car into the crowd or I will kill you now." X drives into the crowd, causing hurt. X may have a defence. Y is told "Commit theft tomorrow or I will kill you next week." The threat is not of instant death – no defence. Z is forced at gunpoint to kill W – murder – no defence.

BNS SECTION 33 - ACT CAUSING SLIGHT HARM
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing is an offence by reason that it causes harm so slight that no person of ordinary sense and temper would complain of such harm.

PROCEDURE: De Minimis Non Curat Lex (The Law Does Not Concern Itself with Trifles)

This is the principle that the law ignores trivial matters

Even if an act technically fits the definition of an offence, it is not punishable if the harm is very slight

The test is objective: Would a person of ordinary sense and temper complain of such harm?

"Person of ordinary sense and temper" means a reasonable, average person

This applies only to harm – not to acts that are serious regardless of harm

KEYWORDS:

de minimis

slight harm

trivial

reasonable person

ordinary sense and temper

ILLUSTRATION:
X touches Y's shoulder to get his attention. Y is not harmed in any way. Even if this technically fits the definition of "criminal force" (touching without consent), no offence because the harm is so slight that no reasonable person would complain. X pushes Y causing Y to fall and break his arm – serious harm – section does not apply.

BNS SECTION 34 - THINGS DONE IN PRIVATE DEFENCE
CORE CONTENT:
Nothing is an offence which is done in the exercise of the right of private defence.

PROCEDURE: Right of Private Defence

This section introduces the right of private defence (detailed in sections 35-44)

Any act done in the lawful exercise of private defence is not an offence

The right of private defence is a justification, not an excuse

The right is available against aggressors and wrongdoers

The right is subject to limitations in sections 37-43

KEYWORDS:

private defence

self-defence

defence of property

justification

not an offence

ILLUSTRATION:
X attacks Y with a knife. Y, in reasonable fear of death, hits X with a stick to stop the attack. Y's act is done in private defence and is not an offence.

BNS SECTION 35 - RIGHT OF PRIVATE DEFENCE OF BODY AND OF PROPERTY
CORE CONTENT:
Every person has a right to defend his own body and the body of any other person against any offence affecting the human body, and the property of himself or any other person against theft, robbery, mischief, criminal trespass, or attempt to commit these offences.

PROCEDURE: Scope of Private Defence

The right extends to:

(i) Defence of one's own body against offences affecting the body

(ii) Defence of another person's body (third-party defence)

(iii) Defence of one's own property against theft, robbery, mischief, criminal trespass, or attempt thereof

(iv) Defence of another person's property (third-party property defence)

The right is available against the specific offences listed

The right is available even if the aggressor is a public servant acting unlawfully

KEYWORDS:

private defence

defence of body

defence of property

third-party defence

theft

robbery

mischief

criminal trespass

ILLUSTRATION:
X sees Y attacking Z with a stick. X can use reasonable force to defend Z's body. X sees Y stealing Z's car. X can use reasonable force to stop the theft and defend Z's property.

BNS SECTION 36 - RIGHT OF PRIVATE DEFENCE AGAINST ACT OF PERSON OF UNSOUND MIND
CORE CONTENT:
When an act which would otherwise be an offence is not an offence by reason of youth, want of maturity of understanding, unsoundness of mind, intoxication, or misconception of the person doing that act, every person has the same right of private defence against that act as if the act were that offence.

PROCEDURE: Defence Against Persons Not Criminally Liable

A person may need to defend himself against someone who is not criminally liable due to:

(i) Youth (child under 7 or immature child 7-12)

(ii) Want of maturity of understanding

(iii) Unsoundness of mind (insanity)

(iv) Involuntary intoxication

(v) Mistake of fact

Even though the aggressor may not be criminally liable, the defender still has the right of private defence

The defender can use the same level of force as if the aggressor's act were an actual offence

KEYWORDS:

private defence

unsound mind

child

intoxication

mistake of fact

defence against non-liable persons

ILLUSTRATION:
X, who is insane, attacks Y with a knife. Y reasonably fears death. Even though X cannot be criminally prosecuted due to insanity, Y still has the right of private defence and can use force (including lethal force if necessary) to defend himself.

BNS SECTION 37 - ACTS AGAINST WHICH THERE IS NO RIGHT OF PRIVATE DEFENCE
CORE CONTENT:
There is no right of private defence against an act which does not reasonably cause apprehension of death or grievous hurt, if done by a public servant acting in good faith under colour of his office, or by direction of such public servant. There is no right of private defence in cases where there is time to have recourse to public authorities. The right of private defence in no case extends to inflicting more harm than necessary for defence.

PROCEDURE: Limitations on Private Defence

Limitation 1 – Public Servants: No right of private defence against a public servant acting in good faith if:

(i) The act does not reasonably cause apprehension of death or grievous hurt

(ii) The public servant is acting under colour of his office

(iii) The public servant is acting in good faith

Limitation 2 – Recourse to Authorities: If there is time to seek help from police or public authorities, private defence is not available

Limitation 3 – Proportionality: The force used in private defence must not be more than reasonably necessary

KEYWORDS:

private defence

limitations

public servant

good faith

recourse to authorities

proportionality

ILLUSTRATION:
A police officer in good faith tries to arrest X with a valid warrant. X knows the arrest is lawful but resists. No right of private defence because no apprehension of death or grievous hurt. X sees Y stealing his bicycle. Police station is 500 meters away and open. X has time to call police – he should seek their help, not take the law into his own hands.

BNS SECTION 38 - WHEN RIGHT OF PRIVATE DEFENCE OF BODY EXTENDS TO CAUSING DEATH
CORE CONTENT:
The right of private defence of the body extends to causing death or any other harm to the assailant if the offence is: assault causing apprehension of death, assault causing apprehension of grievous hurt, assault with intention of committing rape, assault with intention of gratifying unnatural lust, assault with intention of kidnapping or abducting, assault with intention of wrongfully confining a person where he cannot have recourse to public authorities, or act of throwing or administering acid causing apprehension of grievous hurt.

PROCEDURE: When Lethal Force is Permitted in Self-Defence

Causing death in private defence is permitted only for the following offences:

(i) Assault causing reasonable apprehension of death

(ii) Assault causing reasonable apprehension of grievous hurt

(iii) Assault with intention to commit rape

(iv) Assault with intention to gratify unnatural lust

(v) Assault with intention to kidnap or abduct

(vi) Assault with intention to wrongfully confine where recourse to public authorities is not available

(vii) Act of throwing or administering acid causing apprehension of grievous hurt

For all other offences, causing death in private defence is not permitted (section 39 applies)

KEYWORDS:

private defence

causing death

self-defence

rape

kidnapping

acid attack

grievous hurt

ILLUSTRATION:
X attacks Y with a knife, causing Y reasonable apprehension of death. Y picks up a stone and hits X on the head, killing X. Y has not committed an offence. X attempts to rape Y. Y reasonably fears for her safety and kills X in the struggle. Y has not committed an offence.

BNS SECTION 39 - WHEN RIGHT OF PRIVATE DEFENCE EXTENDS TO CAUSING HARM OTHER THAN DEATH
CORE CONTENT:
If the offence is not of the descriptions specified in section 38, the right of private defence of the body does not extend to voluntary causing of death, but extends to voluntary causing of any harm other than death.

PROCEDURE: Non-Lethal Force in Self-Defence

For offences NOT listed in section 38, the defender cannot cause death

However, the defender can cause any other harm (including grievous hurt) that is reasonably necessary

Example offences: assault without fear of death/grievous hurt, simple hurt, wrongful restraint, etc.

The harm caused must still be proportionate to the harm threatened

KEYWORDS:

private defence

causing harm

non-lethal force

proportionality

grievous hurt

ILLUSTRATION:
X attempts to wrongfully restrain Y (not listed in section 38). Y cannot kill X to prevent this. However, Y can push X, hit X, or use other force (even causing grievous hurt if necessary) to free himself. Y kills X – Y would be guilty of culpable homicide or murder.

BNS SECTION 40 - COMMENCEMENT AND CONTINUANCE OF RIGHT OF PRIVATE DEFENCE OF BODY
CORE CONTENT:
The right of private defence of the body commences as soon as a reasonable apprehension of danger to the body arises from an attempt or threat to commit the offence, and continues as long as such apprehension of danger continues.

PROCEDURE: Timing of Private Defence (Body)

The right begins when:

(i) A reasonable apprehension of danger arises

(ii) From an attempt to commit an offence OR a threat to commit an offence

(iii) Not necessary that the offence has actually been committed or attempted

The right continues as long as:

(i) The reasonable apprehension of danger continues

(ii) Once the danger passes, the right ends

The defender does not have to wait for the first blow

KEYWORDS:

private defence

commencement

continuance

reasonable apprehension

threat

attempt

ILLUSTRATION:
X picks up a knife and moves toward Y, shouting "I will kill you." Y reasonably apprehends danger. Y can use private defence immediately – he does not have to wait for X to actually strike. X runs away. The apprehension of danger ends. Y cannot chase X and attack him – the right has ended.

BNS SECTION 41 - WHEN RIGHT OF PRIVATE DEFENCE OF PROPERTY EXTENDS TO CAUSING DEATH
CORE CONTENT:
The right of private defence of property extends to causing death or any other harm to the wrong-doer if the offence is robbery, house-breaking after sunset and before sunrise, mischief by fire or explosive substance on a building used as human dwelling or place for custody of property, or theft, mischief, or house-trespass causing apprehension that death or grievous hurt will be consequence if right of private defence is not exercised.

PROCEDURE: When Lethal Force is Permitted in Defence of Property

Causing death in defence of property is permitted for:

(i) Robbery

(ii) House-breaking between sunset and sunrise

(iii) Mischief by fire or explosive on a dwelling house or property storage building

(iv) Theft, mischief, or house-trespass that creates reasonable apprehension that death or grievous hurt will result if the right is not exercised

For all other property offences, causing death is not permitted

KEYWORDS:

private defence

defence of property

causing death

robbery

house-breaking

mischief by fire

dwelling house

ILLUSTRATION:
X commits robbery on Y's house at night, threatening to kill Y. Y shoots and kills X in defence of his property. Y has not committed an offence. Z steals a bicycle from a locked shed during the day. The owner cannot kill Z to stop the theft – the offence does not fall under section 41.

BNS SECTION 42 - WHEN RIGHT OF PRIVATE DEFENCE OF PROPERTY EXTENDS TO CAUSING HARM OTHER THAN DEATH
CORE CONTENT:
If the offence is theft, mischief, or criminal trespass not of the descriptions specified in section 41, the right of private defence does not extend to causing death, but extends to causing any harm other than death.

PROCEDURE: Non-Lethal Force in Defence of Property

For offences NOT listed in section 41 (theft, mischief, criminal trespass not involving fear of death/grievous hurt), the defender cannot cause death

However, the defender can cause any other harm (including grievous hurt) that is reasonably necessary

The harm must be proportionate to the harm threatened or the value of the property

KEYWORDS:

private defence

defence of property

non-lethal force

theft

mischief

criminal trespass

ILLUSTRATION:
X is stealing a bicycle from Y's garage during the day. Y cannot kill X, but can push X, tackle X, or use reasonable force (even causing grievous hurt) to stop the theft. Y shoots X – Y would be guilty of culpable homicide or murder.

BNS SECTION 43 - COMMENCEMENT AND CONTINUANCE OF RIGHT OF PRIVATE DEFENCE OF PROPERTY
CORE CONTENT:
The right of private defence of property commences when reasonable apprehension of danger commences. Against theft, it continues till the offender has effected his retreat or property has been recovered. Against robbery, it continues as long as the offender causes or attempts to cause death, hurt, or wrongful restraint. Against criminal trespass or mischief, it continues as long as the offender continues in commission.

PROCEDURE: Timing of Private Defence (Property)

Commencement: When reasonable apprehension of danger begins (not when the offence is actually committed)

Continuance against theft: Until the thief has run away OR the property has been recovered

Continuance against robbery: As long as the robber is causing or attempting to cause death, hurt, or wrongful restraint

Continuance against criminal trespass or mischief: As long as the offender is continuing the trespass or mischief

KEYWORDS:

private defence

defence of property

commencement

continuance

theft

robbery

criminal trespass

mischief

ILLUSTRATION:
X sees Y attempting to break into his shed. Right of private defence commences immediately. Y steals a laptop and runs. Y has retreated. The right ends. X cannot chase Y to recover the laptop. However, if Y is still on the property with the laptop, X can use force to recover it.

BNS SECTION 44 - RIGHT OF PRIVATE DEFENCE AGAINST DEADLY ASSAULT WITH RISK OF HARM TO INNOCENT PERSON
CORE CONTENT:
If in the exercise of the right of private defence against an assault causing apprehension of death, the defender cannot exercise that right without risk of harm to an innocent person, his right of private defence extends to running that risk.

PROCEDURE: Defence When Innocent Persons May Be Harmed

This section applies when the defender is facing a deadly assault (reasonable apprehension of death)

The defender may be forced to take action that risks harming an innocent person

The defender is still permitted to exercise his right of private defence and may run the risk of harming the innocent person

This acknowledges that in extreme situations, the defender may have no safe option

This does not mean the defender can intentionally harm innocent persons – only that he may run the risk

KEYWORDS:

private defence

deadly assault

innocent person

risk of harm

necessity

ILLUSTRATION:
X is in a crowded market. Y throws a grenade toward X. X's only way to protect himself is to kick the grenade away, which may hit an innocent bystander. X can take that action even though it risks harming an innocent person.

BNS SECTION 45 - ABETMENT OF A THING
CORE CONTENT:
A person abets the doing of a thing who instigates any person to do that thing, or engages with one or more persons in any conspiracy for doing that thing if an act or illegal omission takes place in pursuance of that conspiracy, or intentionally aids by any act or illegal omission the doing of that thing.

PROCEDURE: Three Modes of Abetment

Mode 1 – Instigation: Intentionally encouraging, urging, or provoking another person to do the act

Mode 2 – Conspiracy: Engaging in a conspiracy to do the thing, provided an act or illegal omission takes place in pursuance of that conspiracy

Mode 3 – Intentional Aid: Intentionally helping the doing of the thing by an act or illegal omission

KEYWORDS:

abetment

instigation

conspiracy

intentional aid

illegal omission

accomplice

ILLUSTRATION:
X tells Y "Go kill Z." X has abetted by instigation. X and Y agree to kill Z, and X buys the weapon for Y. X has abetted by conspiracy. X knows Y is going to kill Z and knowingly leaves the door unlocked so Y can enter. X has abetted by intentional aid.

BNS SECTION 46 - ABETTOR
CORE CONTENT:
A person abets an offence who abets either the commission of an offence or the commission of an act which would be an offence if committed by a person capable by law of committing an offence with the same intention or knowledge as that of the abettor. To constitute abetment, it is not necessary that the act abetted should be committed.

PROCEDURE: When Abetment Occurs

Abetment can be of:

(i) The commission of an actual offence, OR

(ii) An act that would be an offence if done by a person capable of committing it (e.g., abetting a child to do something)

The abettor's intention or knowledge is the relevant factor

The act abetted does NOT need to be actually committed for abetment to exist

Abetment is a separate offence from the main offence

KEYWORDS:

abettor

abetment

completed offence

attempted abetment

intention

knowledge

ILLUSTRATION:
X instigates Y, a 10-year-old child, to commit theft. Even though Y cannot be prosecuted for theft (due to age), X can be prosecuted for abetment. X instigates Y to kill Z. Y changes his mind and does not kill Z. X can still be prosecuted for abetment because the act need not be committed.

BNS SECTION 47 - ABETMENT IN INDIA OF OFFENCES OUTSIDE INDIA
CORE CONTENT:
A person abets an offence who, in India, abets the commission of any act without and beyond India which would constitute an offence if committed in India.

PROCEDURE: Extraterritorial Abetment

This section applies when the abetment occurs in India

The act abetted occurs outside India

That act, if committed in India, would be an offence under BNS

The abettor can be prosecuted in India even though the main act occurred abroad

KEYWORDS:

abetment

extraterritorial jurisdiction

India

outside India

cross-border crime

ILLUSTRATION:
X is in India. He instigates Y (in Dubai) to commit murder of Z (in Dubai). Murder is an offence under BNS if committed in India. X can be prosecuted in India for abetment of murder.

BNS SECTION 48 - ABETMENT OUTSIDE INDIA FOR OFFENCE IN INDIA
CORE CONTENT:
A person abets an offence who, without and beyond India, abets the commission of any act in India which would constitute an offence if committed in India.

PROCEDURE: Abetment from Outside India

This section applies when the abetment occurs outside India

The act abetted occurs in India

That act, if committed in India, is an offence under BNS

The abettor can be prosecuted in India even though he was outside India when he abetted

KEYWORDS:

abetment

extraterritorial jurisdiction

outside India

India

cross-border crime

ILLUSTRATION:
X is in Dubai. He instigates Y (in India) to commit murder of Z (in India). Murder is an offence under BNS. X can be prosecuted in India for abetment of murder.

BNS SECTION 49 - PUNISHMENT OF ABETMENT IF ACT ABETTED IS COMMITTED
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever abets any offence shall, if the act abetted is committed in consequence of the abetment, and no express provision is made for punishment of such abetment, be punished with the punishment provided for the offence.

PROCEDURE: Punishment When Abetted Act is Committed

Applies when:

(i) The abetment occurred

(ii) The act abetted was actually committed as a consequence of the abetment

(iii) No specific punishment for abetment is provided in the section creating the offence

The abettor receives the SAME punishment as the principal offender

KEYWORDS:

abetment

punishment

principal offender

same punishment

consequence

ILLUSTRATION:
X instigates Y to commit theft. Y commits theft. Theft is punishable with 3 years imprisonment. X shall also be punished with 3 years imprisonment (the same punishment as Y).

BNS SECTION 50 - PUNISHMENT OF ABETMENT IF PERSON ABETTED DOES ACT WITH DIFFERENT INTENTION
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever abets the commission of an offence shall, if the person abetted does the act with a different intention or knowledge from that of the abettor, be punished with the punishment provided for the offence which would have been committed if the act had been done with the intention or knowledge of the abettor.

PROCEDURE: Punishment When Different Intention

Applies when:

(i) The abettor has a certain intention or knowledge

(ii) The person abetted does the act but with a DIFFERENT intention or knowledge

The abettor is punished for the offence that would have occurred if his intention had been carried out

This may result in a higher punishment for the abettor than for the actual offender

KEYWORDS:

abetment

different intention

different knowledge

abettor's intention

punishment

ILLUSTRATION:
X instigates Y to cause grievous hurt to Z (intending grievous hurt). Y only intends to cause simple hurt and does so. X is punished for abetment of grievous hurt (the offence he intended), not simple hurt. The punishment for grievous hurt (7 years) may be higher than for simple hurt (1 year).

BNS SECTION 51 - LIABILITY OF ABETTOR WHEN ONE ACT ABETTED AND DIFFERENT ACT DONE
CORE CONTENT:
When an act is abetted and a different act is done, the abettor is liable for the act done, if the act done was a probable consequence of the abetment and was committed under the influence of the instigation or in pursuance of the conspiracy.

PROCEDURE: Extended Liability for Probable Consequences

Applies when:

(i) Act A was abetted

(ii) Act B (different from Act A) was actually done

(iii) Act B was a probable consequence of the abetment

(iv) Act B was committed under the influence of the instigation or in pursuance of the conspiracy

The abettor is liable for Act B

This extends the abettor's liability beyond what he specifically intended

KEYWORDS:

abetment

different act

probable consequence

extended liability

instigation

conspiracy

ILLUSTRATION:
X instigates Y to assault Z (simple hurt). Y, in the course of the assault, kills Z. Death was a probable consequence of an assault. X is liable for abetment of culpable homicide (or murder) even though he only instigated assault.

BNS SECTION 52 - ABETTOR LIABLE TO CUMULATIVE PUNISHMENT FOR ACT ABETTED AND FOR ACT DONE
CORE CONTENT:
If the act for which the abettor is liable is committed in addition to the act abetted and constitutes a distinct offence, the abettor is liable to punishment for each of the offences.

PROCEDURE: Cumulative Punishment for Abettor

Applies when:

(i) The abettor is liable for both the act abetted (Act A) and the act done (Act B)

(ii) Act A and Act B are distinct offences

The abettor can be punished for BOTH offences

The sentences may run consecutively or concurrently at the Court's discretion

KEYWORDS:

abetment

cumulative punishment

distinct offence

separate offences

multiple liability

ILLUSTRATION:
X instigates Y to commit theft (Act A). Y commits theft and also assaults Z during the theft (Act B). X is liable for abetment of theft AND abetment of assault. X can be punished for both offences.

BNS SECTION 53 - LIABILITY OF ABETTOR FOR EFFECT CAUSED DIFFERENT FROM THAT INTENDED
CORE CONTENT:
When an act is abetted with intention of causing a particular effect, and causes a different effect, the abettor is liable for the effect caused if he knew that the act abetted was likely to cause that effect.

PROCEDURE: Liability for Different Effect

Applies when:

(i) Abettor intended to cause Effect A

(ii) The act caused Effect B (different from Effect A)

(iii) The abettor KNEW that the act was likely to cause Effect B

The abettor is liable for Effect B

This requires knowledge, not just intention

KEYWORDS:

abetment

different effect

knowledge

likely consequence

liability

ILLUSTRATION:
X instigates Y to throw a stone at Z, intending only to cause minor hurt. X knows that throwing a stone at Z's head is likely to cause serious injury. The stone hits Z's head and causes grievous hurt. X is liable for grievous hurt because he knew it was likely.

BNS SECTION 54 - ABETTOR PRESENT WHEN OFFENCE IS COMMITTED
CORE CONTENT:
Whenever any person who would be liable to be punished as an abettor if absent, is present when the act or offence is committed, he shall be deemed to have committed such act or offence.

PROCEDURE: Abettor Present Deemed Principal Offender

Applies when:

(i) A person would be liable as an abettor

(ii) That person is present at the time the offence is committed

That person is deemed to have committed the act or offence himself

This means they can be charged as the principal offender, not just as an abettor

This may affect the sentence (principal offenders may receive higher sentences)

KEYWORDS:

abettor

present

principal offender

deemed to have committed

constructive liability

ILLUSTRATION:
X instigates Y to assault Z. X stands nearby watching as Y assaults Z. X is present when the offence is committed. X can be charged as the principal offender (as if he himself assaulted Z), not just as an abettor.

BNS SECTION 55 - ABETMENT OF OFFENCE PUNISHABLE WITH DEATH OR IMPRISONMENT FOR LIFE
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever abets an offence punishable with death or imprisonment for life, if that offence is not committed, shall be punished with imprisonment up to seven years and fine. If hurt is caused, the abettor shall be liable to imprisonment up to fourteen years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Punishment for Abetment of Serious Offences When Offence Not Committed

Applies when:

(i) The offence abetted is punishable with death or imprisonment for life

(ii) The offence is NOT actually committed

Standard punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

Enhanced punishment (if hurt is caused): Imprisonment up to 14 years + fine

This is higher than the general abetment provision in section 56

KEYWORDS:

abetment

death penalty

imprisonment for life

offence not committed

hurt caused

enhanced punishment

ILLUSTRATION:
X instigates Y to murder Z. Y changes his mind and does not commit murder. X is punishable with up to 7 years imprisonment. X instigates Y to murder Z. Y attempts to murder Z and causes hurt but Z survives. X is punishable with up to 14 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 56 - ABETMENT OF OFFENCE PUNISHABLE WITH IMPRISONMENT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever abets an offence punishable with imprisonment, if that offence is not committed, shall be punished with imprisonment up to one-fourth of the longest term provided for that offence, or fine, or both. If the abettor is a public servant whose duty is to prevent the offence, he shall be punished with imprisonment up to one-half of the longest term.

PROCEDURE: Punishment for Abetment of Imprisonable Offences When Offence Not Committed

Applies when:

(i) The offence abetted is punishable with imprisonment (not death or life)

(ii) The offence is NOT actually committed

Standard punishment: Imprisonment up to one-fourth of maximum term, OR fine, OR both

Enhanced punishment (public servant whose duty is to prevent the offence): Imprisonment up to one-half of maximum term

KEYWORDS:

abetment

imprisonment

offence not committed

public servant

enhanced punishment

ILLUSTRATION:
X abets Y to commit theft (max punishment 3 years). Y does not commit theft. X is punishable with up to 9 months imprisonment (one-fourth of 3 years). If X is a police officer whose duty is to prevent theft, X is punishable with up to 18 months imprisonment (one-half of 3 years).

BNS SECTION 57 - ABETTING COMMISSION OF OFFENCE BY PUBLIC OR MORE THAN TEN PERSONS
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever abets the commission of an offence by the public generally or by any number or class of persons exceeding ten, shall be punished with imprisonment up to seven years and with fine.

PROCEDURE: Punishment for Mass Abetment

Applies when the abetment is directed at:

(i) The public generally (mass public), OR

(ii) A number or class of persons exceeding ten

This covers incitement to riot, public mischief, etc.

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

This is regardless of whether the offence is actually committed

KEYWORDS:

abetment

public generally

mass incitement

riot

more than ten persons

ILLUSTRATION:
X stands in a public square and shouts "Everyone attack the police station!" More than ten people respond. X has abetted the commission of an offence by the public generally and is punishable with up to 7 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 58 - CONCEALING DESIGN TO COMMIT OFFENCE PUNISHABLE WITH DEATH OR IMPRISONMENT FOR LIFE
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever intending to facilitate the commission of an offence punishable with death or imprisonment for life, voluntarily conceals the existence of a design to commit such offence, shall if the offence be committed be punished with imprisonment up to seven years, or if not committed with imprisonment up to three years, and shall also be liable to fine.

PROCEDURE: Concealment of Design for Serious Offences

Applies when:

(i) The intended offence is punishable with death or imprisonment for life

(ii) The person voluntarily conceals the existence of a design to commit that offence

(iii) The intention is to facilitate the commission of the offence

If the offence is committed: Punishment up to 7 years + fine

If the offence is not committed: Punishment up to 3 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

concealment

design to commit offence

death penalty

imprisonment for life

facilitate

ILLUSTRATION:
X knows that Y is planning to murder Z. X intentionally does not inform the police to help Y. Y murders Z. X is punishable with up to 7 years imprisonment. If Y does not commit murder, X is punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 59 - PUBLIC SERVANT CONCEALING DESIGN TO COMMIT OFFENCE WHICH IT IS HIS DUTY TO PREVENT
CORE CONTENT:
Being a public servant, if the offence is committed, he shall be punished with imprisonment up to one-half of the longest term. If the offence is punishable with death or imprisonment for life, he shall be punished with imprisonment up to ten years. If the offence is not committed, he shall be punished with imprisonment up to one-fourth of the longest term.

PROCEDURE: Public Servant Concealment Enhanced Punishment

Applies to public servants whose duty is to prevent the offence

If the offence is committed (general): Punishment up to one-half of maximum term

If the offence is punishable with death or life imprisonment (and committed): Punishment up to 10 years

If the offence is not committed: Punishment up to one-fourth of maximum term

KEYWORDS:

public servant

concealment

duty to prevent

enhanced punishment

death penalty

ILLUSTRATION:
X is a police officer whose duty is to prevent murder. He knows Y plans to murder Z and conceals this. Y murders Z. X is punishable with up to 10 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 60 - CONCEALING DESIGN TO COMMIT OFFENCE PUNISHABLE WITH IMPRISONMENT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever conceals design to commit an offence punishable with imprisonment shall, if the offence be committed, be punished with imprisonment up to one-fourth, and if not committed up to one-eighth, of the longest term of imprisonment, or with fine, or with both.

PROCEDURE: Concealment of Design for Imprisonable Offences

Applies when the intended offence is punishable with imprisonment (not death or life)

If the offence is committed: Punishment up to one-fourth of maximum term OR fine OR both

If the offence is not committed: Punishment up to one-eighth of maximum term OR fine OR both

KEYWORDS:

concealment

design to commit offence

imprisonment

fractions

ILLUSTRATION:
X knows Y plans to commit theft (max punishment 3 years). X conceals this. Y commits theft. X is punishable with up to 9 months (one-fourth of 3 years). If Y does not commit theft, X is punishable with up to 4.5 months (one-eighth of 3 years).

BNS SECTION 61 - CRIMINAL CONSPIRACY
CORE CONTENT:
When two or more persons agree with the common object to do an illegal act, or an act which is not illegal by illegal means, such agreement is designated a criminal conspiracy. Whoever is party to a criminal conspiracy to commit an offence punishable with death, imprisonment for life, or rigorous imprisonment for two years or upwards, shall be punished in the same manner as if he had abetted such offence. Other criminal conspiracies shall be punished with imprisonment up to six months, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Criminal Conspiracy

Essential elements:

(i) Two or more persons

(ii) Agreement

(iii) Common object to do an illegal act OR to do a legal act by illegal means

The agreement itself is the offence – no need for any further act

Serious conspiracies (for offences punishable with death/life/2+ years rigorous imprisonment): Punished as abetment of that offence

Other conspiracies: Imprisonment up to 6 months OR fine OR both

KEYWORDS:

criminal conspiracy

agreement

common object

illegal act

illegal means

conspiracy punishment

ILLUSTRATION:
X and Y agree to murder Z (punishable with death). They have committed criminal conspiracy and are punishable as if they abetted murder. A and B agree to commit a minor public nuisance (punishable with fine). They are punishable with up to 6 months imprisonment or fine.

BNS SECTION 62 - PUNISHMENT FOR ATTEMPTING TO COMMIT OFFENCES PUNISHABLE WITH IMPRISONMENT FOR LIFE OR OTHER IMPRISONMENT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever attempts to commit an offence punishable with imprisonment for life or imprisonment, and does any act towards the commission of the offence, shall be punished with imprisonment up to one-half of the imprisonment for life or one-half of the longest term of imprisonment provided for the offence, or with fine, or with both.

PROCEDURE: Attempt Punishment

Essential elements:

(i) Intention to commit the offence

(ii) An act done towards the commission of the offence (more than mere preparation)

(iii) The act falls short of the complete offence

For attempt of life imprisonment: Imprisonment up to 10 years (one-half of 20 years calculation)

For attempt of term imprisonment: Imprisonment up to one-half of maximum term, or fine, or both

KEYWORDS:

attempt

preparation

act towards commission

attempted murder

half punishment

ILLUSTRATION:
X shoots at Y intending to kill Y. The bullet misses. X has attempted murder (punishable with life imprisonment) and is punishable with up to 10 years imprisonment. X picks up a stone to throw at Y with intent to cause hurt but is caught before throwing. This may be only preparation, not attempt.

BNS SECTION 63 - RAPE
CORE CONTENT:
A man is said to commit rape if he penetrates his penis to any extent into the vagina, mouth, urethra or anus of a woman or makes her to do so; inserts any object or part of the body not being the penis into the vagina, urethra or anus of a woman; manipulates any part of the body of a woman so as to cause penetration; or applies his mouth to the vagina, anus, urethra of a woman, under seven circumstances: against her will, without her consent, consent obtained by fear of death or hurt, consent given believing he is another man she is lawfully married to, consent given when she is unable to understand consequences due to unsoundness of mind or intoxication, with or without consent when she is under eighteen years, or when she is unable to communicate consent.

PROCEDURE: Elements of Rape

Acts constituting rape:

(i) Penile penetration of vagina, mouth, urethra, or anus to any extent

(ii) Insertion of any object or body part (not penis) into vagina, urethra, or anus

(iii) Manipulation of body causing penetration

(iv) Application of mouth to vagina, anus, or urethra

The act must fall under any of seven circumstances:

(a) Against her will

(b) Without her consent

(c) Consent obtained by fear of death or hurt

(d) Consent given believing she is lawfully married to another man

(e) Consent given when unable to understand consequences (unsound mind/intoxication)

(f) With or without consent if she is under 18 years (statutory rape)

(g) When she is unable to communicate consent

Penetration to any extent is sufficient – full penetration not required

KEYWORDS:

rape

penetration

consent

without consent

statutory rape

minor

sexual assault

ILLUSTRATION:
X forces Y to have sexual intercourse against her will. X has committed rape. X has sexual intercourse with Y, a 17-year-old, with her consent. X has still committed rape because Y is under 18 (statutory rape).

BNS SECTION 64 - PUNISHMENT FOR RAPE
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever commits rape shall be punished with rigorous imprisonment not less than ten years but which may extend to imprisonment for life, and shall also be liable to fine. Enhanced punishment applies to police officers, public servants, armed forces members, jail staff, hospital staff, relatives, guardians, teachers, persons in position of trust, during communal violence, when the woman is pregnant, incapable of giving consent, mentally or physically disabled, or when rape causes grievous bodily harm or is committed repeatedly on the same woman.

PROCEDURE: Rape Punishment

Standard punishment: Rigorous imprisonment 10 years minimum – life maximum + fine

Enhanced punishment circumstances (among others):

(i) Police officer, public servant, armed forces member committing rape

(ii) Jail or hospital staff committing rape

(iii) Relative, guardian, teacher, person in position of trust

(iv) During communal or sectarian violence

(v) When the woman is pregnant

(vi) When the woman is incapable of giving consent

(vii) When the woman is mentally or physically disabled

(viii) When rape causes grievous bodily harm

(ix) When rape is committed repeatedly on the same woman

For enhanced circumstances, punishment may be higher (see sections 65, 66, 70, 71)

KEYWORDS:

rape punishment

rigorous imprisonment

imprisonment for life

enhanced punishment

position of trust

communal violence

ILLUSTRATION:
X commits rape on Y. X is punishable with rigorous imprisonment of at least 10 years. If X is a police officer who commits rape, he is subject to enhanced punishment (up to life imprisonment meaning remainder of natural life).

BNS SECTION 65 - PUNISHMENT FOR RAPE IN CERTAIN CASES
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever commits rape on a woman under sixteen years of age shall be punished with rigorous imprisonment not less than twenty years which may extend to imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life) and fine. Rape on a woman under twelve years of age shall be punished with rigorous imprisonment not less than twenty years which may extend to imprisonment for life or with death, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Rape of Minor – Enhanced Punishment

Victim under 16 years:

Minimum 20 years rigorous imprisonment

Maximum: Imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life)

Fine

Victim under 12 years:

Minimum 20 years rigorous imprisonment

Maximum: Imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life) OR Death

Fine

KEYWORDS:

rape of minor

under sixteen

under twelve

death penalty

imprisonment for life

rigorous imprisonment

ILLUSTRATION:
X commits rape on a 15-year-old girl. X is punishable with minimum 20 years rigorous imprisonment. X commits rape on a 10-year-old girl. X is punishable with minimum 20 years rigorous imprisonment up to death sentence.

BNS SECTION 66 - PUNISHMENT FOR CAUSING DEATH OR RESULTING IN PERSISTENT VEGETATIVE STATE OF VICTIM
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever commits an offence punishable under section 64 and in the course of such commission inflicts an injury which causes the death of the woman or causes her to be in a persistent vegetative state, shall be punished with rigorous imprisonment not less than twenty years which may extend to imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life), or with death.

PROCEDURE: Rape Resulting in Death or PVS

Applies when:

(i) Rape is committed (under section 64)

(ii) During the rape, an injury is inflicted

(iii) That injury causes death OR causes the woman to be in a persistent vegetative state

Punishment: Minimum 20 years rigorous imprisonment

Maximum: Imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life) OR Death

KEYWORDS:

rape causing death

persistent vegetative state

death sentence

imprisonment for life

rigorous imprisonment

ILLUSTRATION:
X rapes Y and during the rape, strangles Y causing her death. X is punishable with minimum 20 years rigorous imprisonment up to death sentence. X rapes Y and inflicts a head injury that puts Y into a permanent vegetative state. Same punishment applies.

BNS SECTION 67 - SEXUAL INTERCOURSE BY HUSBAND UPON HIS WIFE DURING SEPARATION
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever has sexual intercourse with his own wife, who is living separately, whether under a decree of separation or otherwise, without her consent, shall be punished with imprisonment not less than two years but which may extend to seven years, and shall also be liable to fine.

PROCEDURE: Marital Rape During Separation

Applies only when:

(i) The couple is married

(ii) The wife is living separately (under decree of separation OR otherwise)

(iii) The husband has sexual intercourse with her

(iv) WITHOUT her consent

Not applicable to married couples living together

Punishment: Imprisonment 2 years minimum – 7 years maximum + fine

KEYWORDS:

marital rape

separation

judicial separation

without consent

husband

ILLUSTRATION:
X and Y are married but living separately under a court decree. X forces Y to have sexual intercourse. X has committed an offence punishable with 2-7 years imprisonment. If X and Y are living together as husband and wife, this section does not apply.

BNS SECTION 68 - SEXUAL INTERCOURSE BY A PERSON IN AUTHORITY
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever being in a position of authority or fiduciary relationship, a public servant, superintendent or manager of a jail or institution, or on management of a hospital, abuses such position to induce or seduce any woman in his custody or under his charge to have sexual intercourse with him, not amounting to rape, shall be punished with rigorous imprisonment not less than five years but which may extend to ten years, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Abuse of Authority for Sexual Favours

Applies to:

(i) Persons in position of authority or fiduciary relationship

(ii) Public servants

(iii) Jail/institution superintendents or managers

(iv) Hospital management personnel

The offender must abuse his position to induce or seduce a woman in his custody/charge

The act must NOT amount to rape (otherwise section 64 applies)

Punishment: Rigorous imprisonment 5 years minimum – 10 years maximum + fine

KEYWORDS:

abuse of authority

sexual intercourse

position of authority

fiduciary relationship

public servant

custody

ILLUSTRATION:
X is a jail superintendent. He tells a female prisoner that he will give her special privileges if she has sexual intercourse with him. She consents due to the inducement. X has committed an offence punishable with 5-10 years rigorous imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 69 - SEXUAL INTERCOURSE BY EMPLOYING DECEITFUL MEANS
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever, by deceitful means or by making promise to marry to a woman without any intention of fulfilling the same, has sexual intercourse with her, not amounting to rape, shall be punished with imprisonment up to ten years and fine. Deceitful means includes inducement for false promise of employment or promotion, or marrying by suppressing identity.

PROCEDURE: Sexual Intercourse by Deceit

Applies when:

(i) The man uses deceitful means OR makes a false promise of marriage without intention to fulfill

(ii) He has sexual intercourse with the woman

(iii) The act does NOT amount to rape (otherwise section 64 applies)

Deceitful means includes:

(i) False promise of employment

(ii) False promise of promotion

(iii) Marrying by suppressing identity

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

deceitful means

false promise of marriage

suppressed identity

sexual intercourse

cheating

ILLUSTRATION:
X promises to marry Y, has sexual intercourse with her, and then refuses to marry. X never intended to marry. X has committed an offence punishable with up to 10 years imprisonment. X marries Y suppressing the fact that he is already married. The marriage is void and the intercourse may be covered under this section.

BNS SECTION 70 - GANG RAPE
CORE CONTENT:
Where a woman is raped by one or more persons constituting a group or acting in furtherance of common intention, each of those persons shall be deemed to have committed rape and shall be punished with rigorous imprisonment not less than twenty years which may extend to imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life), and fine. Where a woman under eighteen years is gang raped, each person shall be punished with imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life) and fine, or death.

PROCEDURE: Gang Rape Punishment

Essential elements:

(i) Rape is committed

(ii) By one or more persons constituting a group OR acting in furtherance of common intention

(iii) Each person is deemed to have committed rape

Standard punishment (victim any age): Rigorous imprisonment 20 years minimum – life (remainder of natural life) maximum + fine

Victim under 18 years: Imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life) OR death + fine

KEYWORDS:

gang rape

common intention

group rape

death penalty

imprisonment for life

minor victim

ILLUSTRATION:
X, Y, and Z together rape W. Each of X, Y, and Z is punishable with minimum 20 years rigorous imprisonment up to life imprisonment. If W is 16 years old, X, Y, and Z are each punishable with life imprisonment or death.

BNS SECTION 71 - PUNISHMENT FOR REPEAT OFFENDERS
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever has been previously convicted of an offence punishable under section 64, 65, 66, or 70 and is subsequently convicted of an offence punishable under any of the said sections shall be punished with imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life), or with death.

PROCEDURE: Repeat Sexual Offender Punishment

Applies when:

(i) Previous conviction under section 64, 65, 66, or 70

(ii) Subsequent conviction under any of the same sections

Punishment: Imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life) OR Death

No minimum sentence – the Court may choose between life and death

This is the most severe punishment for repeat sexual offenders

KEYWORDS:

repeat offender

previous conviction

subsequent conviction

death penalty

imprisonment for life

habitual sexual offender

ILLUSTRATION:
X was previously convicted for rape under section 64 and served 10 years. After release, X again commits rape under section 64. On the second conviction, X shall be punished with imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life) or death.

BNS SECTION 72 - DISCLOSURE OF IDENTITY OF VICTIM OF CERTAIN OFFENCES
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever prints or publishes the name or any matter which may make known the identity of a person against whom an offence under sections 64 to 71 is alleged or found to have been committed, shall be punished with imprisonment up to two years and fine. Exceptions include publication by police for investigation, by the victim, or by next of kin of deceased or child victim to recognised welfare institutions.

PROCEDURE: Victim Identity Protection

Prohibited: Printing or publishing name or identifying matter of victim of offences under sections 64-71

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 2 years + fine

Exceptions:

(i) Publication by police for investigation purposes

(ii) Publication by the victim herself

(iii) Publication by next of kin of deceased victim or child victim to recognised welfare institutions

KEYWORDS:

victim identity

publication

privacy

rape victim

sexual assault victim

media restrictions

ILLUSTRATION:
A newspaper publishes the name and photograph of a rape victim without her consent. The newspaper can be prosecuted and the editor punished with up to 2 years imprisonment. The police may release identifying information to help locate a suspect.

BNS SECTION 73 - PRINTING OR PUBLISHING MATTER RELATING TO COURT PROCEEDINGS WITHOUT PERMISSION
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever prints or publishes any matter in relation to any proceeding before a Court with respect to an offence referred to in section 72 without the previous permission of such Court shall be punished with imprisonment up to two years and fine. Printing of judgment of High Court or Supreme Court does not amount to an offence.

PROCEDURE: Court Proceedings Confidentiality

Applies to proceedings regarding offences under sections 64-71

No one may print or publish matter relating to such proceedings without Court permission

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 2 years + fine

Exception: Printing of High Court or Supreme Court judgments is permitted

KEYWORDS:

court proceedings

publication

permission of court

in-camera trial

judgment publication

ILLUSTRATION:
A journalist attends an in-camera rape trial and publishes details of the victim's testimony without Court permission. The journalist is punishable with up to 2 years imprisonment. The same journalist may publish the High Court's judgment after appeal.

BNS SECTION 74 - ASSAULT OR USE OF CRIMINAL FORCE TO WOMAN WITH INTENT TO OUTRAGE HER MODESTY
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever assaults or uses criminal force to any woman, intending to outrage or knowing it to be likely that he will thereby outrage her modesty, shall be punished with imprisonment not less than one year but which may extend to five years, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Assault to Outrage Modesty

Essential elements:

(i) Assault OR use of criminal force to a woman

(ii) Intention to outrage her modesty OR knowledge that it is likely to outrage her modesty

Punishment: Imprisonment 1 year minimum – 5 years maximum + fine

"Outraging modesty" is interpreted based on social norms and the specific circumstances

Physical contact is not necessary – a gesture or act can constitute assault

KEYWORDS:

assault

criminal force

outraging modesty

woman

sexual harassment

ILLUSTRATION:
X grabs Y's breast without her consent. X has outraged her modesty and is punishable with 1-5 years imprisonment. X makes lewd gestures at Y and touches her inappropriately – same section applies.

BNS SECTION 75 - SEXUAL HARASSMENT
CORE CONTENT:
A man committing physical contact and advances involving unwelcome explicit sexual overtures, demand or request for sexual favours, showing pornography against the will of a woman, or making sexually coloured remarks, shall be guilty of sexual harassment. Punishment for overtures, demand for favours, or showing pornography is rigorous imprisonment up to three years, or fine, or both. Punishment for sexually coloured remarks is imprisonment up to one year, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Sexual Harassment

Acts constituting sexual harassment:

(i) Physical contact and advances involving unwelcome explicit sexual overtures

(ii) Demand or request for sexual favours

(iii) Showing pornography against the will of a woman

(iv) Making sexually coloured remarks

Punishment for (i), (ii), (iii): Rigorous imprisonment up to 3 years OR fine OR both

Punishment for (iv) sexually coloured remarks: Imprisonment up to 1 year OR fine OR both

KEYWORDS:

sexual harassment

unwelcome sexual overtures

sexual favours

pornography

sexually coloured remarks

workplace harassment

ILLUSTRATION:
X repeatedly asks his female colleague for sexual favours. X is guilty of sexual harassment and punishable with up to 3 years rigorous imprisonment. X makes sexually coloured jokes about a female coworker. Punishable with up to 1 year imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 76 - ASSAULT OR USE OF CRIMINAL FORCE TO WOMAN WITH INTENT TO DISROBE
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever assaults or uses criminal force to any woman or abets such act with the intention of disrobing or compelling her to be naked, shall be punished with imprisonment not less than three years but which may extend to seven years, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Assault to Disrobe

Essential elements:

(i) Assault OR use of criminal force to a woman OR abetment of such act

(ii) Intention to disrobe her OR compel her to be naked

Punishment: Imprisonment 3 years minimum – 7 years maximum + fine

KEYWORDS:

disrobing

assault

criminal force

woman

naked

abetment

ILLUSTRATION:
X grabs Y's clothes and tries to tear them off to make her naked. X has committed an offence punishable with 3-7 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 77 - VOYEURISM
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever watches or captures the image of a woman engaging in a private act where she would have expectation of not being observed, or disseminates such image, shall be punished on first conviction with imprisonment not less than one year but up to three years and fine, and on second or subsequent conviction with imprisonment not less than three years but up to seven years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Voyeurism

Acts constituting voyeurism:

(i) Watching a woman engaging in a private act

(ii) Capturing the image of a woman engaging in a private act

(iii) Disseminating such image

The woman must have a reasonable expectation of not being observed

First conviction: Imprisonment 1 year minimum – 3 years maximum + fine

Second/subsequent conviction: Imprisonment 3 years minimum – 7 years maximum + fine

KEYWORDS:

voyeurism

BNS SECTION 100 - CULPABLE HOMICIDE
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever causes death by doing an act with the intention of causing death, or with the intention of causing such bodily injury as is likely to cause death, or with the knowledge that he is likely by such act to cause death, commits the offence of culpable homicide.

PROCEDURE: Culpable Homicide Definition

Three ways to commit culpable homicide:

(i) Intention to cause death AND death results

(ii) Intention to cause bodily injury likely to cause death AND death results

(iii) Knowledge that the act is likely to cause death AND death results

Culpable homicide is the broader category. Murder is a subset of culpable homicide (when certain aggravating conditions are present – see section 101)

Distinction between culpable homicide and murder depends on the degree of intention/knowledge and the probability of death

KEYWORDS:

culpable homicide

intention to cause death

bodily injury likely to cause death

knowledge likely to cause death

death

ILLUSTRATION:
X shoots Y intending to kill Y. Y dies. X has committed culpable homicide (which may be murder depending on circumstances). X hits Y with a stick knowing that such a blow to the head is likely to cause death. Y dies. Culpable homicide. X drives recklessly knowing that it is likely to cause death. Y dies. Culpable homicide.

BNS SECTION 101 - MURDER
CORE CONTENT:
Culpable homicide is murder if the act by which death is caused is done with the intention of causing death, or with the intention of causing such bodily injury as the offender knows to be likely to cause death, or with the intention of causing bodily injury sufficient in the ordinary course of nature to cause death, or if the person knows that the act is so imminently dangerous that it must in all probability cause death or such injury as is likely to cause death.

PROCEDURE: When Culpable Homicide Becomes Murder

Four clauses defining murder:

(1) Intention to cause death – If the offender intends to cause death and death results, it is murder

(2) Intention to cause injury known to be likely to cause death – The offender intends to cause bodily injury, and knows that such injury is likely to cause death

(3) Intention to cause injury sufficient in ordinary course to cause death – The offender intends to cause bodily injury, and that injury is sufficient in the ordinary course of nature to cause death

(4) Imminently dangerous act – The offender knows that the act is so imminently dangerous that it must in all probability cause death or such injury as is likely to cause death (e.g., shooting into a crowd)

The distinction between culpable homicide not amounting to murder and murder depends on the degree of probability of death and the nature of the intention

Exceptions to murder (culpable homicide not amounting to murder) are covered in section 105

KEYWORDS:

murder

intention to cause death

bodily injury likely to cause death

sufficient in ordinary course of nature

imminently dangerous act

culpable homicide

ILLUSTRATION:
X shoots Y in the chest intending to kill Y. Y dies. Murder under clause (1). X stabs Y in the stomach intending to cause serious injury, knowing that such injury is likely to cause death. Y dies. Murder under clause (2). X fires a gun into a crowded market knowing that it will likely kill someone. A bystander dies. Murder under clause (4).

BNS SECTION 102 - CULPABLE HOMICIDE BY CAUSING DEATH OF PERSON OTHER THAN WHOSE DEATH WAS INTENDED
CORE CONTENT:
If a person by doing anything which he intends or knows to be likely to cause death, commits culpable homicide by causing the death of any person whose death he neither intends nor knows himself to be likely to cause, the culpable homicide committed is of the description it would have been if he had caused the death of the person whose death he intended.

PROCEDURE: Transferred Intent (Doctrine of Transfer Malice)

Essential principle: The intention follows the act

If A intends to kill B but accidentally kills C, A is guilty of the same offence as if he had killed B

The intention to kill B is "transferred" to the actual victim C

This applies to both murder and culpable homicide not amounting to murder

The nature of the offence (murder vs. culpable homicide) depends on A's intention towards the intended victim

KEYWORDS:

transferred intent

transfer of malice

intended victim

actual victim

culpable homicide

murder

ILLUSTRATION:
X intends to kill Y and shoots at Y. The bullet misses Y and hits Z, killing Z. X is guilty of murder of Z as if he had killed Y. X intends to cause grievous hurt to Y (not death) but knows the blow to the head is likely to cause death. The blow misses Y and kills Z instead. X is guilty of culpable homicide of Z (not murder) because his intention towards Y was not to kill.

BNS SECTION 103 - PUNISHMENT FOR MURDER
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever commits murder shall be punished with death or imprisonment for life, and shall also be liable to fine. When a group of five or more persons acting in concert commits murder on the ground of race, caste, community, sex, place of birth, language, personal belief or any other similar ground, each member of such group shall be punished with death or imprisonment for life, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Murder Punishment

Standard murder: Death OR imprisonment for life + fine

Aggravated murder (group of 5+ persons acting in concert on discriminatory grounds):

(i) Grounds: race, caste, community, sex, place of birth, language, personal belief, or similar grounds

(ii) Each member of the group is punishable with death or imprisonment for life + fine

The Court has discretion to choose between death and life imprisonment

For life imprisonment, "imprisonment for life" means the remainder of the convict's natural life for certain aggravated cases (section 104)

KEYWORDS:

fine

ILLUSTRATION:

BNS SECTION 104 - PUNISHMENT FOR MURDER BY LIFE-CONVICT
CORE CONTENT:

PROCEDURE: Murder Committed by Life Convict

Applies to persons already serving a life sentence

If such person commits murder, punishment is:

(i) Death, OR

(ii) Imprisonment for the remainder of that person's natural life (not just 20 or 14 years)

This section removes the possibility of remission or early release

BNS SECTION 105 - PUNISHMENT FOR CULPABLE HOMICIDE NOT AMOUNTING TO MURDER
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever commits culpable homicide not amounting to murder, if the act is done with intention of causing death or bodily injury likely to cause death, shall be punished with imprisonment for life, or imprisonment not less than five years but which may extend to ten years, and fine. If the act is done with knowledge that it is likely to cause death but without any intention to cause death, he shall be punished with imprisonment up to ten years, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Two Categories of Culpable Homicide Not Amounting to Murder

Category A (Intention to cause death or injury likely to cause death):

Punishment: Imprisonment for life OR imprisonment 5 years minimum – 10 years maximum + fine

Category B (Knowledge that act is likely to cause death, but no intention to cause death):

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 10 years OR fine OR both

KEYWORDS:

ILLUSTRATION:
X and Y get into a fight. X picks up a stick and hits Y on the head, intending to cause serious injury. X knows that such a blow is likely to cause death. Y dies. X is guilty of culpable homicide (Category A) and punishable with 5-10 years imprisonment or life. Z drives recklessly at high speed knowing it is likely to cause death but not intending to kill. A pedestrian dies. Z is guilty of culpable homicide (Category B) and punishable with up to 10 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 106 - CAUSING DEATH BY NEGLIGENCE
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever causes death by doing any rash or negligent act not amounting to culpable homicide, shall be punished with imprisonment up to five years and fine. If such act is done by a registered medical practitioner while performing medical procedure, he shall be punished with imprisonment up to two years and fine. Whoever causes death by rash and negligent driving of vehicle and escapes without reporting it soon after the incident, shall be punished with imprisonment up to ten years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Negligent Death

Standard negligent death: Imprisonment up to 5 years + fine

Medical negligence causing death: Imprisonment up to 2 years + fine

Hit-and-run causing death: Imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

Distinction from culpable homicide: Here, there is no intention to cause death and no knowledge that death is likely – only rashness or negligence

"Rash" means acting without due caution (taking a known risk)

"Negligent" means failing to exercise reasonable care

KEYWORDS:

causing death by negligence

rash act

negligent act

medical negligence

hit and run

culpable homicide distinction

ILLUSTRATION:
X drives at normal speed but fails to notice a red light due to distraction and hits a pedestrian, causing death. X is guilty of causing death by negligence (not culpable homicide) and punishable with up to 5 years imprisonment. A doctor makes a surgical error that causes death (no criminal intent). Punishable with up to 2 years imprisonment. X hits a pedestrian with his car, causing death, and flees without reporting. Punishable with up to 10 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 107 - ABETMENT OF SUICIDE OF CHILD OR PERSON OF UNSOUND MIND
CORE CONTENT:
If any child, person of unsound mind, delirious person or person in a state of intoxication commits suicide, whoever abets such suicide shall be punished with death or imprisonment for life, or imprisonment not exceeding ten years, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Abetment of Suicide of Vulnerable Persons

Applies when the person who commits suicide is:

(i) A child

(ii) A person of unsound mind

(iii) A delirious person

(iv) A person in a state of intoxication

Punishment: Death OR imprisonment for life OR imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

This is more severe than abetment of suicide of a normal person (section 108)

KEYWORDS:

abetment of suicide

child

unsound mind

delirious person

intoxication

death penalty

ILLUSTRATION:
X instigates Y, a 10-year-old child, to commit suicide. Y commits suicide. X is punishable with death, life imprisonment, or up to 10 years imprisonment and fine.

BNS SECTION 108 - ABETMENT OF SUICIDE
CORE CONTENT:
If any person commits suicide, whoever abets the commission of such suicide shall be punished with imprisonment up to ten years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Abetment of Suicide

Essential elements:

(i) A person commits suicide

(ii) Another person abetted the commission of that suicide

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

"Abetment" includes instigation, conspiracy, or intentional aid (as defined in sections 45-46)

KEYWORDS:

abetment of suicide

suicide

instigation

conspiracy

intentional aid

ILLUSTRATION:
X constantly taunts and humiliates Y, telling Y that he is worthless and should kill himself. Y commits suicide. X may be guilty of abetment of suicide and punishable with up to 10 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 109 - ATTEMPT TO MURDER
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever does any act with such intention or knowledge that if he caused death he would be guilty of murder, shall be punished with imprisonment up to ten years and fine. If hurt is caused to any person by such act, the offender shall be liable to imprisonment for life, or to such punishment as hereinbefore mentioned. When the offender is under sentence of imprisonment for life, he may, if hurt is caused, be punished with death or imprisonment for the remainder of his natural life.

PROCEDURE: Attempt to Murder

Essential elements:

(i) An act done (more than mere preparation)

(ii) Intention or knowledge that if death resulted, it would be murder

(iii) Death does not actually occur

Standard punishment (no hurt caused): Imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

Enhanced punishment (hurt caused): Imprisonment for life OR punishment as above

If the offender is a life convict and hurt is caused: Death OR imprisonment for remainder of natural life

KEYWORDS:

attempt to murder

act towards commission

hurt caused

imprisonment for life

death penalty

life convict

ILLUSTRATION:
X shoots at Y intending to kill Y. The bullet misses. X is guilty of attempt to murder and punishable with up to 10 years imprisonment. X shoots Y and Y is seriously injured but survives. X is punishable with imprisonment for life (or up to 10 years). X is serving a life sentence and attempts to murder a prison guard, causing injury. X may be sentenced to death or imprisonment for remainder of natural life.

BNS SECTION 110 - ATTEMPT TO COMMIT CULPABLE HOMICIDE
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever does any act with such intention or knowledge that if he caused death he would be guilty of culpable homicide not amounting to murder, shall be punished with imprisonment up to three years, or fine, or both. If hurt is caused to any person, he shall be punished with imprisonment up to seven years, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Attempt to Commit Culpable Homicide

Essential elements:

(i) An act done (more than mere preparation)

(ii) Intention or knowledge that if death resulted, it would be culpable homicide not amounting to murder

(iii) Death does not actually occur

Standard punishment (no hurt caused): Imprisonment up to 3 years OR fine OR both

Enhanced punishment (hurt caused): Imprisonment up to 7 years OR fine OR both

KEYWORDS:

attempt to commit culpable homicide

intention

knowledge

hurt caused

culpable homicide not amounting to murder

ILLUSTRATION:
X hits Y with a stick intending to cause serious injury (not death) but knowing it could cause death. Y survives without serious injury. X is guilty of attempt to commit culpable homicide and punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment. If Y is seriously injured, X is punishable with up to 7 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 111 - ORGANISED CRIME
CORE CONTENT:
Any continuing unlawful activity including kidnapping, robbery, vehicle theft, extortion, land grabbing, contract killing, economic offence, cyber-crimes, trafficking of persons, drugs, weapons or illicit goods, by any person or group acting in concert as member of an organised crime syndicate, using violence or threat to obtain material benefit, constitutes organised crime. Punishment if death results is death or imprisonment for life and fine not less than ten lakh rupees. In any other case, punishment is imprisonment not less than five years which may extend to imprisonment for life and fine not less than five lakh rupees.

PROCEDURE: Organised Crime

Essential elements:

(i) Continuing unlawful activity (not a one-time act)

(ii) By a person or group acting in concert as member of organised crime syndicate

(iii) Using violence or threat to obtain material benefit

Types of activities covered: kidnapping, robbery, vehicle theft, extortion, land grabbing, contract killing, economic offences, cyber-crimes, trafficking of persons, drugs, weapons, illicit goods

If death results: Death OR imprisonment for life + fine (minimum ₹10 lakh)

In any other case: Imprisonment 5 years minimum – life maximum + fine (minimum ₹5 lakh)

KEYWORDS:

organised crime

organised crime syndicate

continuing unlawful activity

contract killing

trafficking

death penalty

fine

ILLUSTRATION:
X is a member of a syndicate that carries out extortion, kidnapping, and contract killings on a regular basis. The syndicate kills a victim who refused to pay. X is punishable with death or life imprisonment and fine of at least ₹10 lakh.

BNS SECTION 112 - PETTY ORGANISED CRIME
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever, being a member of a group or gang, commits any act of theft, snatching, cheating, unauthorised selling of tickets, unauthorised betting or gambling, selling of public examination question papers or any other similar criminal act, commits petty organised crime. Punishment is imprisonment not less than one year but which may extend to seven years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Petty Organised Crime

Essential elements:

(i) Member of a group or gang

(ii) Commits specified criminal acts: theft, snatching, cheating, unauthorised ticket selling, unauthorised betting/gambling, selling exam question papers, or similar criminal acts

Punishment: Imprisonment 1 year minimum – 7 years maximum + fine

KEYWORDS:

petty organised crime

group

gang

theft

snatching

cheating

ticket black market

exam paper leak

ILLUSTRATION:
X is a member of a gang that steals mobile phones from passengers on crowded trains. X is guilty of petty organised crime and punishable with 1-7 years imprisonment. Y is part of a group that sells leaked exam papers. Y is similarly punishable.

BNS SECTION 113 - TERRORIST ACT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever does any act with intent to threaten the unity, integrity, sovereignty, security or economic security of India or to strike terror in the people, by using bombs, dynamite, explosive substances, firearms, lethal weapons, poisonous gases, or other hazardous substances causing death, injury, loss or damage to property, disruption of essential supplies, damage to monetary stability through counterfeit currency, or overawes by criminal force, or detains any person to compel government action, commits a terrorist act. Punishment if death results is death or imprisonment for life and fine. In any other case, imprisonment not less than five years which may extend to imprisonment for life and fine.

PROCEDURE: Terrorist Act

Essential elements:

(i) Act done with intent to threaten unity, integrity, sovereignty, security, or economic security of India OR to strike terror in the people

(ii) Using: bombs, dynamite, explosive substances, firearms, lethal weapons, poisonous gases, hazardous substances, or other means

(iii) Causing death, injury, loss/damage to property, disruption of essential supplies, damage to monetary stability, or overawing by criminal force, or detention to compel government action

If death results: Death OR imprisonment for life + fine

In any other case: Imprisonment 5 years minimum – life maximum + fine

KEYWORDS:

terrorist act

threat to sovereignty

terror

bomb

explosive

firearm

hazardous substance

death penalty

ILLUSTRATION:
X plants a bomb in a crowded market intending to strike terror in the people. The bomb explodes, killing 10 people. X is punishable with death or life imprisonment and fine.

BNS SECTION 114 - HURT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever causes bodily pain, disease or infirmity to any person is said to cause hurt.

PROCEDURE: Hurt Definition

Three ways to cause hurt:

(i) Causing bodily pain (any level of pain)

(ii) Causing disease (any illness)

(iii) Causing infirmity (physical weakness or incapacity)

This is the definition section – punishment is under subsequent sections

KEYWORDS:

hurt

bodily pain

disease

infirmity

definition

ILLUSTRATION:
X slaps Y causing pain. X has caused hurt. X infects Y with a disease. X has caused hurt. X breaks Y's leg causing infirmity. X has caused hurt.

BNS SECTION 115 - VOLUNTARILY CAUSING HURT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever does any act with the intention of causing hurt to any person, or with the knowledge that he is likely thereby to cause hurt, and does thereby cause hurt, is said voluntarily to cause hurt. Punishment is imprisonment up to one year, or fine up to ten thousand rupees, or both.

PROCEDURE: Voluntarily Causing Hurt

Essential elements:

(i) An act done

(ii) Intention to cause hurt OR knowledge that hurt is likely

(iii) Hurt is actually caused

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 1 year OR fine up to ₹10,000 OR both

KEYWORDS:

voluntarily causing hurt

intention

knowledge

hurt

punishment

ILLUSTRATION:
X punches Y in the face intending to cause pain. Y suffers pain and bruising. X is punishable with up to 1 year imprisonment or fine up to ₹10,000. X throws a stone into a crowd knowing it is likely to hit someone. The stone hits Z causing hurt. X is punishable under this section.

BNS SECTION 116 - GRIEVOUS HURT
CORE CONTENT:
The following kinds of hurt are designated as grievous: emasculation, permanent privation of sight of either eye, permanent privation of hearing of either ear, privation of any member or joint, destruction or permanent impairing of powers of any member or joint, permanent disfiguration of head or face, fracture or dislocation of a bone or tooth, and any hurt which endangers life or causes severe bodily pain for fifteen days or inability to follow ordinary pursuits.

PROCEDURE: Grievous Hurt Definition

Eight categories of grievous hurt:

(i) Emasculation (loss of male reproductive organs)

(ii) Permanent privation of sight (any eye)

(iii) Permanent privation of hearing (any ear)

(iv) Privation of any member or joint (loss of any limb or joint)

(v) Destruction or permanent impairing of powers of any member or joint

(vi) Permanent disfiguration of head or face

(vii) Fracture or dislocation of a bone or tooth

(viii) Any hurt that endangers life OR causes severe bodily pain for 15 days OR inability to follow ordinary pursuits for 15 days

This is the definition section – punishment is under section 117

KEYWORDS:

grievous hurt

emasculation

permanent privation

fracture

disfigurement

endanger life

inability to follow ordinary pursuits

ILLUSTRATION:
X breaks Y's arm (fracture). This is grievous hurt. X causes injuries that require Y to stay in bed for 20 days (unable to follow ordinary pursuits). This is grievous hurt.

BNS SECTION 117 - VOLUNTARILY CAUSING GRIEVOUS HURT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever voluntarily causes hurt which he intends or knows to be likely to cause grievous hurt, and causes grievous hurt, is said voluntarily to cause grievous hurt. Punishment is imprisonment up to seven years and fine. Where it causes permanent disability or persistent vegetative state, punishment is rigorous imprisonment not less than ten years which may extend to imprisonment for life (remainder of natural life). When a group of five or more persons acting in concert causes grievous hurt on ground of race, caste, community, sex, place of birth, language, or personal belief, each member shall be punished with imprisonment up to seven years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Voluntarily Causing Grievous Hurt

Standard grievous hurt: Imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

Aggravated grievous hurt (causing permanent disability or persistent vegetative state): Rigorous imprisonment 10 years minimum – life (remainder of natural life) maximum

Hate crime grievous hurt (group of 5+ persons acting in concert on discriminatory grounds): Each member punishable with imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

voluntarily causing grievous hurt

grievous hurt

permanent disability

persistent vegetative state

hate crime

group violence

ILLUSTRATION:
X breaks Y's arm intentionally. X is punishable with up to 7 years imprisonment. X hits Y on the head, causing permanent brain damage and persistent vegetative state. X is punishable with 10 years minimum rigorous imprisonment up to life imprisonment. A group of 6 persons beats up a person from another community due to caste hatred, causing grievous hurt. Each of the 6 is punishable with up to 7 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 118 - VOLUNTARILY CAUSING HURT OR GRIEVOUS HURT BY DANGEROUS WEAPONS OR MEANS
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever voluntarily causes hurt by means of any instrument for shooting, stabbing or cutting, fire, heated substance, poison, corrosive substance, explosive substance, deleterious substance, or animal, shall be punished with imprisonment up to three years, or fine up to twenty thousand rupees, or both. Whoever voluntarily causes grievous hurt by such means shall be punished with imprisonment for life, or imprisonment not less than one year but which may extend to ten years, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Hurt by Dangerous Means

Hurt by dangerous means: Imprisonment up to 3 years OR fine up to ₹20,000 OR both

Grievous hurt by dangerous means: Imprisonment for life OR imprisonment 1 year minimum – 10 years maximum + fine

Dangerous means include: guns, knives, swords, fire, hot substances, poison, acid, explosives, harmful substances, animals

KEYWORDS:

dangerous weapons

hurt by dangerous means

grievous hurt

firearm

knife

poison

acid

ILLUSTRATION:
X stabs Y with a knife causing simple hurt. X is punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment or fine. X throws acid on Y's face causing permanent disfigurement (grievous hurt). X is punishable with imprisonment for life or 1-10 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 119 - VOLUNTARILY CAUSING HURT OR GRIEVOUS HURT TO EXTORT PROPERTY OR CONSTRAIN TO ILLEGAL ACT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever voluntarily causes hurt for the purpose of extorting property or valuable security, or constraining to do anything illegal, shall be punished with imprisonment up to ten years and fine. Whoever voluntarily causes grievous hurt for such purpose shall be punished with imprisonment for life, or imprisonment up to ten years, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Hurt for Extortion

Hurt for extortion: Imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

Grievous hurt for extortion: Imprisonment for life OR imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

hurt for extortion

BNS SECTION 120 - VOLUNTARILY CAUSING HURT OR GRIEVOUS HURT TO EXTORT CONFESSION OR COMPEL RESTORATION OF PROPERTY
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever voluntarily causes hurt to extort confession or information for detection of offence, or to compel restoration of property, shall be punished with imprisonment up to seven years and fine. Whoever voluntarily causes grievous hurt for such purpose shall be punished with imprisonment up to ten years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Hurt for Confession or Property Restoration

Hurt to extort confession or restore property: Imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

Grievous hurt for same purpose: Imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

BNS SECTION 121 - VOLUNTARILY CAUSING HURT OR GRIEVOUS HURT TO DETER PUBLIC SERVANT FROM DUTY
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever voluntarily causes hurt to a public servant in discharge of his duty, or to deter him from discharging his duty, shall be punished with imprisonment up to five years, or fine, or both. Whoever voluntarily causes grievous hurt to a public servant for such purpose shall be punished with imprisonment not less than one year but which may extend to ten years, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Hurt to Public Servant

Hurt to public servant: Imprisonment up to 5 years OR fine OR both

Grievous hurt to public servant: Imprisonment 1 year minimum – 10 years maximum + fine

hurt to public servant

BNS SECTION 122 - VOLUNTARILY CAUSING HURT OR GRIEVOUS HURT ON PROVOCATION
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever voluntarily causes hurt on grave and sudden provocation, if he neither intends nor knows himself to be likely to cause hurt to any person other than the person who gave provocation, shall be punished with imprisonment up to one month, or fine up to five thousand rupees, or both. Whoever voluntarily causes grievous hurt on grave and sudden provocation shall be punished with imprisonment up to five years, or fine up to ten thousand rupees, or both.

PROCEDURE: Hurt on Provocation

Hurt on grave and sudden provocation: Imprisonment up to 1 month OR fine up to ₹5,000 OR both

Grievous hurt on grave and sudden provocation: Imprisonment up to 5 years OR fine up to ₹10,000 OR both

"Grave and sudden provocation" means provocation that would cause a reasonable person to lose self-control

The provocation must not be sought or voluntarily caused by the offender

KEYWORDS:

provocation

grave and sudden provocation

hurt on provocation

grievous hurt on provocation

reduced punishment

ILLUSTRATION:
X sees Y assaulting X's wife. X immediately punches Y, causing hurt. X is punishable with up to 1 month imprisonment or fine. X sees Y assaulting X's wife. X picks up a stick and breaks Y's arm (grievous hurt). X is punishable with up to 5 years imprisonment or fine.

BNS SECTION 123 - CAUSING HURT BY MEANS OF POISON WITH INTENT TO COMMIT OFFENCE
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever administers to or causes to be taken by any person any poison or any stupefying, intoxicating or unwholesome drug with intent to cause hurt, or with intent to commit or facilitate commission of an offence, shall be punished with imprisonment up to ten years and fine.

BNS SECTION 124 - VOLUNTARILY CAUSING GRIEVOUS HURT BY USE OF ACID
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever causes permanent or partial damage or deformity, or burns or maims or disfigures or disables any part of the body of a person, or causes grievous hurt by throwing or administering acid, shall be punished with imprisonment not less than ten years which may extend to imprisonment for life and fine. Whoever attempts to throw or administer acid shall be punished with imprisonment not less than five years but which may extend to seven years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Acid Attack

Acid attack causing grievous hurt: Imprisonment 10 years minimum – life maximum + fine

Attempt to throw or administer acid: Imprisonment 5 years minimum – 7 years maximum + fine

BNS SECTION 125 - ACT ENDANGERING LIFE OR PERSONAL SAFETY OF OTHERS

Whoever does any act so rashly or negligently as to endanger human life or personal safety of others, shall be punished with imprisonment up to three months or fine up to two thousand five hundred rupees, or both. Where hurt is caused, imprisonment up to six months or fine up to five thousand rupees, or both. Where grievous hurt is caused, imprisonment up to three years or fine up to ten thousand rupees, or both.

BNS SECTION 126 - WRONGFUL RESTRAINT

BNS SECTION 127 - WRONGFUL CONFINEMENT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever wrongfully restrains any person preventing him from proceeding beyond certain circumscribing limits, is said wrongfully to confine that person. Punishment is imprisonment up to one year, or fine up to five thousand rupees, or both. For confinement for three days or more, imprisonment up to three years, or fine up to ten thousand rupees, or both. For ten days or more, imprisonment up to five years and fine not less than ten thousand rupees.

PROCEDURE: Wrongful Confinement

Essential elements:

BNS SECTION 128 - FORCE
CORE CONTENT:
A person is said to use force to another if he causes motion, change of motion, or cessation of motion to that other, or causes any substance to come into contact with any part of that other's body, by his own bodily power, by disposing any substance, or by inducing any animal to move.

PROCEDURE: Force Definition

Force can be applied in three ways:

(i) By his own bodily power

(ii) By disposing any substance (e.g., throwing a stone)

(iii) By inducing any animal to move

The effect on the other person must be:

(i) Causing motion, or

(ii) Causing change of motion, or

(iii) Causing cessation of motion, or

(iv) Causing any substance to come into contact with any part of that other's body

KEYWORDS:

force

motion

change of motion

cessation of motion

bodily power

ILLUSTRATION:
X pushes Y (bodily power causing motion). X has used force. X throws a stone that hits Y (disposing substance causing contact). X has used force. X commands his dog to attack Y (inducing animal). X has used force.

BNS SECTION 129 - CRIMINAL FORCE
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever intentionally uses force to any person without that person's consent, in order to commit any offence, or intending by such force to cause, or knowing it to be likely that he will cause injury, fear or annoyance, is said to use criminal force.

PROCEDURE: Criminal Force Definition

Essential elements:

(i) Force used intentionally (not accidentally)

(ii) Without the person's consent

(iii) For one of these purposes:

(a) In order to commit any offence, OR

(b) Intending to cause injury, fear, or annoyance, OR

(c) Knowing it is likely to cause injury, fear, or annoyance

This is the definition – punishment is under section 131

KEYWORDS:

criminal force

without consent

intentional force

injury

fear

annoyance

ILLUSTRATION:
X pushes Y without Y's consent, intending to annoy Y. X has used criminal force. X intentionally bumps into Y to pick Y's pocket. X has used criminal force.

BNS SECTION 130 - ASSAULT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever makes any gesture or any preparation intending or knowing it to be likely that such gesture or preparation will cause any person present to apprehend that he is about to use criminal force to that person, is said to commit assault.

PROCEDURE: Assault Definition

Essential elements:

(i) Making a gesture OR making a preparation

(ii) Intention OR knowledge that it is likely to cause apprehension

(iii) A person present apprehends that criminal force is about to be used

No physical contact is required – only apprehension of immediate criminal force

This is the definition – punishment is under section 131

BNS SECTION 131 - PUNISHMENT FOR ASSAULT OR CRIMINAL FORCE OTHERWISE THAN ON GRAVE PROVOCATION
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever assaults or uses criminal force to any person otherwise than on grave and sudden provocation given by that person, shall be punished with imprisonment up to three months, or fine up to one thousand rupees, or both.

PROCEDURE: Assault and Criminal Force Punishment

Applies to assault (section 130) and criminal force (section 129)

Does NOT apply if the act was done on grave and sudden provocation by the victim

BNS SECTION 132 - ASSAULT OR CRIMINAL FORCE TO DETER PUBLIC SERVANT FROM DISCHARGE OF HIS DUTY
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever assaults or uses criminal force to any person being a public servant in the execution of his duty, or with intent to prevent or deter him from discharging his duty, shall be punished with imprisonment up to two years, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Assault on Public Servant

Essential elements:

(i) Assault OR criminal force

(ii) To a person who is a public servant

(iii) The public servant is in execution of his duty OR the act is intended to prevent/deter him from discharging duty

KEYWORDS:

assault on public servant

deter public servant

execution of duty

BNS SECTION 137 - KIDNAPPING
CORE CONTENT:
Kidnapping is of two kinds: kidnapping from India (conveying any person beyond the limits of India without consent), and kidnapping from lawful guardianship (taking or enticing any child or person of unsound mind out of the keeping of lawful guardian without consent). Punishment is imprisonment up to seven years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Kidnapping

Kidnapping from India:

(i) Conveying any person beyond the limits of India

(ii) Without that person's consent

Kidnapping from lawful guardianship:

(i) Taking or enticing a child (under 18 for boys? under 18 for girls? – BNS uses "child" defined as under 18)

(ii) OR person of unsound mind

(iii) Out of the keeping of the lawful guardian

(iv) Without the guardian's consent

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

unsound mind

ILLUSTRATION:

BNS SECTION 138 - ABDUCTION
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever by force compels, or by any deceitful means induces, any person to go from any place, is said to abduct that person.

PROCEDURE: Abduction Definition

Two ways to abduct:

(i) By force compelling any person to go from any place

(ii) By deceitful means inducing any person to go from any place

Unlike kidnapping, abduction does not require the person to be a child or of unsound mind

Consent obtained by force or deceit is not valid consent

This is the definition – punishment is under subsequent sections (140, 143, etc.)

KEYWORDS:

ILLUSTRATION:

BNS SECTION 139 - KIDNAPPING OR MAIMING A CHILD FOR PURPOSES OF BEGGING

BNS SECTION 143 - TRAFFICKING OF PERSON
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever for the purpose of exploitation recruits, transports, harbours, transfers, or receives a person by using threats, force, coercion, abduction, fraud, deception, abuse of power, or inducement, commits trafficking. Punishment is rigorous imprisonment not less than seven years but which may extend to ten years, and fine. Trafficking of more than one person: rigorous imprisonment not less than ten years which may extend to imprisonment for life, and fine. Trafficking of a child: rigorous imprisonment not less than ten years which may extend to imprisonment for life, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Human Trafficking

Means used: threats, force, coercion, abduction, fraud, deception, abuse of power, or inducement

Purpose: exploitation (including sexual exploitation, forced labour, slavery, etc.)

Standard (one adult): Rigorous imprisonment 7 years minimum – 10 years maximum + fine

Trafficking of more than one person: Rigorous imprisonment 10 years minimum – life maximum + fine

Trafficking of a child: Rigorous imprisonment 10 years minimum – life maximum + fine

BNS SECTION 189 - UNLAWFUL ASSEMBLY
CORE CONTENT:
An assembly of five or more persons is designated an unlawful assembly if the common object is to overawe government by criminal force, resist execution of law, commit mischief or criminal trespass, take possession of property by criminal force, or compel any person to do what he is not legally bound to do. Whoever joins or continues in an unlawful assembly shall be punished with imprisonment up to six months, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Unlawful Assembly

Five or more persons assembled

Common object must be one or more of:

(i) Overawe Central/State Government or any public servant by criminal force

(ii) Resist execution of any law or legal process

(iii) Commit mischief or criminal trespass

(iv) Take possession of property by criminal force

(v) Compel any person to do what he is not legally bound to do, or omit to do what he is legally bound to do

Mere presence in an unlawful assembly is an offence

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 6 months OR fine OR both

KEYWORDS:

unlawful assembly

mischief

criminal trespass

ILLUSTRATION:

BNS SECTION 190 - EVERY MEMBER OF UNLAWFUL ASSEMBLY GUILTY OF OFFENCE COMMITTED IN PROSECUTION OF COMMON OBJECT
CORE CONTENT:
If an offence is committed by any member of an unlawful assembly in prosecution of the common object, every person who at the time of committing that offence is a member of the same assembly, is guilty of that offence.

PROCEDURE: Constructive Liability for Unlawful Assembly

Essential elements:

(i) An unlawful assembly exists

(ii) One member commits an offence in prosecution of the common object

(iii) Other members are present at the time

All members of the assembly are guilty of that offence, even if they did not personally commit it

This is a form of constructive criminal liability

KEYWORDS:

unlawful assembly

vicarious liability

ILLUSTRATION:
A group of 10 persons forms an unlawful assembly with common object of attacking a person. One person in the group kills the victim. All 10 persons are guilty of murder, even if only one pulled the trigger.

BNS SECTION 231 - GIVING OR FABRICATING FALSE EVIDENCE WITH INTENT TO PROCURE CONVICTION FOR IMPRISONMENT FOR LIFE OR IMPRISONMENT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever gives or fabricates false evidence intending to cause any person to be convicted of an offence punishable with imprisonment for life or imprisonment for seven years or upwards, shall be punished as a person convicted of that offence would be liable to be punished.

PROCEDURE: False Evidence for Serious Offence

Applies when false evidence is given to procure conviction for:

(i) Offence punishable with imprisonment for life, OR

(ii) Offence punishable with imprisonment for 7 years or more

Punishment: Same as the punishment for the offence the person intended to cause conviction for

This is a "mirror punishment" provision

KEYWORDS:

BNS SECTION 303 - THEFT

BNS SECTION 308 - EXTORTION

Whoever intentionally puts any person in fear of any injury to that person or to any other, and thereby dishonestly induces the person so put in fear to deliver to any person any property or valuable security, commits extortion. BNS SECTION 309 - ROBBERY
CORE CONTENT:
Theft is robbery if in order to commit theft the offender voluntarily causes or attempts to cause death, hurt or wrongful restraint. Extortion is robbery if the offender puts the person in fear of instant death, instant hurt, or instant wrongful restraint. Punishment for robbery is rigorous imprisonment up to ten years and fine. If committed on highway between sunset and sunrise, imprisonment up to fourteen years. Attempt to commit robbery: rigorous imprisonment up to seven years and fine. If hurt is caused in committing or attempting robbery: imprisonment for life or rigorous imprisonment up to ten years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Robbery

Theft becoming robbery: Theft + voluntarily causing/attempting to cause death/hurt/wrongful restraint in order to commit theft

Extortion becoming robbery: Extortion + putting person in fear of instant death/hurt/wrongful restraint

Standard robbery: Rigorous imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

Robbery on highway between sunset and sunrise: Imprisonment up to 14 years

Attempt to commit robbery: Rigorous imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

Robbery causing hurt: Imprisonment for life OR rigorous imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

ILLUSTRATION:

BNS SECTION 310 - DACOITY

When five or more persons conjointly commit or attempt to commit robbery, every person so committing, attempting or aiding, is said to commit dacoity. If any one of five or more persons committing dacoity commits murder, every one shall be punished with death, or imprisonment for life, or rigorous imprisonment not less than ten years, and fine.

Whoever enters into or upon property in possession of another with intent to commit an offence or to intimidate, insult or annoy any person in possession, is said to commit criminal trespass. Punishment is imprisonment up to three months, or fine up to five thousand rupees, or both. House-trespass is committing criminal trespass into any building used as a human dwelling or place of worship. Punishment is imprisonment up to one year, or fine up to five thousand rupees, or both.

PROCEDURE: Criminal Trespass and House-Trespass

Criminal trespass: Entry into property in possession of another with intent to commit offence OR to intimidate/insult/annoy

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 3 months OR fine up to ₹5,000 OR both

House-trespass: Criminal trespass into any building used as human dwelling or place of worship

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 1 year OR fine up to ₹5,000 OR both

KEYWORDS:

criminal trespass

house-trespass

intent to commit offence

intimidate

insult

annoy

ILLUSTRATION:
X enters Y's fenced backyard without permission, intending to annoy Y. X has committed criminal trespass and is punishable with up to 3 months imprisonment. If X enters Y's house with the same intent, house-trespass, punishable with up to 1 year imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 330 - HOUSE-TRESPASS AND HOUSE-BREAKING
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever commits house-trespass having taken precautions to conceal it is said to commit lurking house-trespass. A person commits house-breaking if he effects entrance through passage made by himself, through passage not intended for human entrance, by opening any lock, by using criminal force, or by any passage which he knows to have been fastened. Punishment for lurking house-trespass or house-breaking is imprisonment up to two years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Lurking House-Trespass and House-Breaking

Lurking house-trespass: House-trespass + taking precautions to conceal it

House-breaking: Effecting entrance by:

(i) Passage made by himself

(ii) Passage not intended for human entrance

(iii) Opening any lock

(iv) Using criminal force

(v) Passage known to have been fastened

Punishment for either: Imprisonment up to 2 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

lurking house-trespass

house-breaking

concealment

opening lock

criminal force

ILLUSTRATION:
X waits until night, climbs over the wall of Y's house, and enters through a window that was left open. X has committed lurking house-trespass and is punishable with up to 2 years imprisonment. X picks the lock on Y's door to enter – house-breaking.

BNS SECTION 331 - PUNISHMENT FOR HOUSE-TRESPASS OR HOUSE-BREAKING
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever commits lurking house-trespass or house-breaking after sunset and before sunrise shall be punished with imprisonment up to three years and fine. If in order to commit an offence punishable with imprisonment, imprisonment up to three years and fine. If the offence intended is theft, imprisonment may extend to ten years. If causes hurt or attempts to cause death, imprisonment for life or imprisonment up to ten years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Enhanced Punishment for House-Trespass/House-Breaking

Night-time (sunset to sunrise): Imprisonment up to 3 years + fine

To commit an offence punishable with imprisonment: Imprisonment up to 3 years + fine

Intended offence is theft: Imprisonment may extend to 10 years

Causes hurt or attempts to cause death: Imprisonment for life OR imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

night-time house-trespass

house-breaking

intended theft

causing hurt

attempt to cause death

ILLUSTRATION:
X breaks into Y's house at 2 AM to steal jewellery. X is punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment (night-time) and up to 10 years because the intended offence is theft. If X also injures Y during the break-in, X may be sentenced to life imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 332 - HOUSE-TRESPASS IN ORDER TO COMMIT OFFENCE
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever commits house-trespass in order to commit an offence punishable with death, shall be punished with imprisonment for life or rigorous imprisonment up to ten years and fine. If punishable with imprisonment for life, imprisonment up to ten years and fine. If punishable with imprisonment, imprisonment up to two years and fine.

PROCEDURE: House-Trespass to Commit Offence

Intended offence punishable with death: Imprisonment for life OR rigorous imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

Intended offence punishable with imprisonment for life: Imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

Intended offence punishable with imprisonment: Imprisonment up to 2 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

house-trespass

intended offence

death penalty

imprisonment for life

ILLUSTRATION:
X enters Y's house intending to murder Y (death-punishable offence). X is punishable with life imprisonment or up to 10 years rigorous imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 333 - HOUSE-TRESPASS AFTER PREPARATION FOR HURT, ASSAULT OR WRONGFUL RESTRAINT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever commits house-trespass having made preparation for causing hurt or assaulting any person, shall be punished with imprisonment up to seven years and fine.

PROCEDURE: House-Trespass with Preparation

Essential elements:

(i) Commits house-trespass

(ii) Having made preparation for causing hurt or assaulting any person

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

house-trespass

preparation for hurt

preparation for assault

ILLUSTRATION:
X enters Y's house carrying a stick, intending to beat Y if caught. X is punishable with up to 7 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 334 - DISHONESTLY BREAKING OPEN RECEPTACLE CONTAINING PROPERTY
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever dishonestly or with intent to commit mischief breaks open any closed receptacle which contains property, shall be punished with imprisonment up to two years, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Breaking Open Receptacle

Essential elements:

(i) Dishonestly OR with intent to commit mischief

(ii) Breaks open any closed receptacle containing property

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 2 years OR fine OR both

KEYWORDS:

breaking open receptacle

closed receptacle

dishonest

ILLUSTRATION:
X breaks open Y's locked cupboard to steal the valuables inside. X is punishable with up to 2 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 335 - MAKING A FALSE DOCUMENT
CORE CONTENT:
A person is said to make a false document who dishonestly or fraudulently makes, signs, seals or executes a document with the intention of causing it to be believed that it was made by a person by whom he knows it was not made, or who without lawful authority alters a document in any material part, or who causes any person to sign a document knowing that such person does not know the contents.

PROCEDURE: Making a False Document - Definition

Three ways to make a false document:

(i) Dishonestly/fraudulently makes/signs/seals/executes a document intending it to be believed it was made by someone else (knowing it was not)

(ii) Without lawful authority alters a document in any material part

(iii) Causes any person to sign a document knowing that person does not know the contents

This is the definition – punishment is under forgery sections

KEYWORDS:

false document

forgery

alteration

signature

material part

ILLUSTRATION:
X signs Y's name on a cheque without Y's permission. X has made a false document. X changes the amount on a signed blank cheque from ₹1,000 to ₹10,000 – false document.

BNS SECTION 336 - FORGERY
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever makes any false document with intent to cause damage or injury to the public or to any person, or to support any claim or title, or to cause any person to part with property, or with intent to commit fraud, commits forgery. Punishment is imprisonment up to two years, or fine, or both. If intended to be used for cheating, imprisonment up to seven years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Forgery

Essential elements:

(i) Makes any false document

(ii) With intent: to cause damage/injury, OR support claim/title, OR cause person to part with property, OR commit fraud

Standard forgery: Imprisonment up to 2 years OR fine OR both

Forgery intended to be used for cheating: Imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

forgery

false document

intent to cause damage

support claim

cheating

ILLUSTRATION:
X creates a fake degree certificate to get a job. X has committed forgery and is punishable with up to 2 years imprisonment. If X uses the fake degree to cheat someone of money, punishment up to 7 years.

BNS SECTION 337 - FORGERY OF RECORD OF COURT OR OF PUBLIC REGISTER
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever forges a document purporting to be a record or proceeding of a Court, or an identity document issued by Government including voter identity card or Aadhaar Card, or a register of birth, marriage or burial, or a power of attorney, shall be punished with imprisonment up to seven years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Forgery of Court Record

Types of documents covered:

(i) Record or proceeding of a Court

(ii) Identity document issued by Government (including voter ID, Aadhaar)

(iii) Register of birth, marriage, or burial

(iv) Power of attorney

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

forgery of court record

forged identity document

forged Aadhaar

forged voter ID

forged power of attorney

ILLUSTRATION:
X creates a fake Aadhaar card in someone else's name. X is punishable with up to 7 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 338 - FORGERY OF VALUABLE SECURITY, WILL
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever forges a document which purports to be a valuable security or a will, or an authority to adopt a son, shall be punished with imprisonment for life, or imprisonment up to ten years, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Forgery of Valuable Security or Will

Documents covered:

(i) Valuable security (e.g., shares, bonds, promissory notes)

(ii) Will

(iii) Authority to adopt a son

Punishment: Imprisonment for life OR imprisonment up to 10 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

forgery of valuable security

forged will

authority to adopt

imprisonment for life

ILLUSTRATION:
X forges Y's will, naming himself as the beneficiary. X is punishable with life imprisonment or up to 10 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 339 - HAVING POSSESSION OF FORGED DOCUMENT
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever has in his possession any forged document described in section 337 or 338, knowing it to be forged and intending to use it as genuine, shall be punished with imprisonment up to seven years and fine, or if under section 338, with imprisonment for life or imprisonment up to seven years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Possession of Forged Document

Essential elements:

(i) Possession of forged document (as described in section 337 or 338)

(ii) Knows it is forged

(iii) Intends to use it as genuine

Forged document under section 337: Imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

Forged document under section 338 (valuable security/will): Imprisonment for life OR imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

possession of forged document

knowledge

intent to use

forged will

ILLUSTRATION:
X has a forged Aadhaar card in his possession (section 337 document), knowing it is forged and intending to use it. X is punishable with up to 7 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 340 - FORGED DOCUMENT AND USING IT AS GENUINE
CORE CONTENT:
A false document made wholly or in part by forgery is designated a forged document. Whoever fraudulently or dishonestly uses as genuine any forged document, shall be punished in the same manner as if he had forged such document.

PROCEDURE: Using Forged Document

Definition: Forged document = false document made wholly or partly by forgery

Offence: Fraudulently or dishonestly using as genuine any forged document

Punishment: Same as for forging that document (sections 336-338)

KEYWORDS:

forged document

using forged document

same punishment as forgery

ILLUSTRATION:
X does not forge a document himself but receives a forged cheque from Y and cashes it, knowing it is forged. X is punishable as if he had forged the cheque himself.

BNS SECTION 341 - MAKING OR POSSESSING COUNTERFEIT SEAL
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever makes or counterfeits any seal, plate or other instrument for making an impression, intending that it shall be used for committing forgery punishable under section 338, shall be punished with imprisonment for life, or imprisonment up to seven years, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Making Counterfeit Seal

Essential elements:

(i) Makes or counterfeits any seal, plate, or instrument for making impression

(ii) Intends it to be used for forgery under section 338 (valuable security/will)

Punishment: Imprisonment for life OR imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

counterfeit seal

counterfeit plate

forgery instrument

imprisonment for life

ILLUSTRATION:
X creates a fake company seal to forge share certificates (valuable security). X is punishable with life imprisonment or up to 7 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 342 - COUNTERFEITING DEVICE OR MARK USED FOR AUTHENTICATING DOCUMENTS
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever counterfeits any device or mark used for authenticating any document described in section 338, intending that such device or mark shall be used for giving appearance of authenticity to any forged document, shall be punished with imprisonment for life, or imprisonment up to seven years, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Counterfeiting Authentication Device

Essential elements:

(i) Counterfeits any device or mark used for authenticating documents

(ii) Document is described in section 338 (valuable security/will)

(iii) Intends it to be used to give appearance of authenticity to forged document

Punishment: Imprisonment for life OR imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

counterfeit authentication device

authentication mark

forged document

imprisonment for life

ILLUSTRATION:
X creates a fake embossing seal used by a bank to authenticate cheques, intending to use it to make fake cheques look genuine. X is punishable with life imprisonment or up to 7 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 343 - FRAUDULENT CANCELLATION, DESTRUCTION OF WILL OR VALUABLE SECURITY
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever fraudulently or dishonestly cancels, destroys or defaces any document which is or purports to be a will, or an authority to adopt a son, or any valuable security, shall be punished with imprisonment for life, or imprisonment up to seven years, and fine.

PROCEDURE: Destruction of Will

Documents covered:

(i) Will

(ii) Authority to adopt a son

(iii) Valuable security

Acts covered: fraudulently or dishonestly cancelling, destroying, or defacing

Punishment: Imprisonment for life OR imprisonment up to 7 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

destruction of will

cancellation of will

defacing will

destruction of valuable security

ILLUSTRATION:
X, the sole beneficiary under Y's will, destroys the will so that no one can claim under it. X is punishable with life imprisonment or up to 7 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 344 - FALSIFICATION OF ACCOUNTS
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever, being a clerk, officer or servant, wilfully and with intent to defraud, destroys, alters, mutilates or falsifies any book, electronic record, paper, valuable security or account which belongs to his employer, shall be punished with imprisonment up to seven years, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Falsification of Accounts

Essential elements:

(i) Person is a clerk, officer, or servant

(ii) Wilfully and with intent to defraud

(iii) Destroys, alters, mutilates, or falsifies

(iv) Any book, electronic record, paper, valuable security, or account belonging to employer

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years OR fine OR both

KEYWORDS:

falsification of accounts

destruction of records

alteration

employee fraud

ILLUSTRATION:
X, an accountant at a company, alters the company's financial records to steal money. X is punishable with up to 7 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 345 - PROPERTY MARK
CORE CONTENT:
A mark used for denoting that movable property belongs to a particular person is called a property mark. Whoever uses any false property mark shall, unless he proves that he acted without intent to defraud, be punished with imprisonment up to one year, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Property Mark

Definition: Mark used to denote ownership of movable property

Offence: Using any false property mark

Burden of proof: Accused must prove he acted without intent to defraud

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 1 year OR fine OR both

KEYWORDS:

property mark

false property mark

intent to defraud

burden of proof

ILLUSTRATION:
X stamps his own brand name on goods that were actually manufactured by Y, intending to pass them off as his own. X is punishable with up to 1 year imprisonment unless he proves no intent to defraud.

BNS SECTION 346 - TAMPERING WITH PROPERTY MARK WITH INTENT TO CAUSE INJURY
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever removes, destroys, defaces or adds to any property mark, intending to cause injury to any person, shall be punished with imprisonment up to one year, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Tampering with Property Mark

Essential elements:

(i) Removes, destroys, defaces, or adds to any property mark

(ii) Intends to cause injury to any person

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 1 year OR fine OR both

KEYWORDS:

tampering with property mark

removal of mark

defacing mark

intent to cause injury

ILLUSTRATION:
X scratches off the serial number on Y's laptop to prevent Y from identifying it. X is punishable with up to 1 year imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 347 - COUNTERFEITING A PROPERTY MARK
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever counterfeits any property mark used by any other person shall be punished with imprisonment up to two years, or fine, or both. If it is a property mark used by a public servant, imprisonment up to three years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Counterfeiting Property Mark

Standard counterfeiting of property mark: Imprisonment up to 2 years OR fine OR both

If property mark used by public servant: Imprisonment up to 3 years + fine

KEYWORDS:

counterfeiting property mark

public servant property mark

ILLUSTRATION:
X creates fake gold hallmark stamps to put on fake gold jewellery. X is punishable with up to 2 years imprisonment. If X counterfeits a government verification stamp, up to 3 years.

BNS SECTION 348 - MAKING OR POSSESSION OF INSTRUMENT FOR COUNTERFEITING A PROPERTY MARK
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever makes or has in his possession any die, plate or other instrument for the purpose of counterfeiting a property mark, shall be punished with imprisonment up to three years, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Possessing Property Mark Counterfeiting Instrument

Essential elements:

(i) Makes or possesses any die, plate, or other instrument

(ii) For the purpose of counterfeiting a property mark

Punishment: Imprisonment up to 3 years OR fine OR both

KEYWORDS:

counterfeiting instrument

die

property mark

X has a machine that can stamp fake brand logos on products. X is punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 349 - SELLING GOODS MARKED WITH A COUNTERFEIT PROPERTY MARK
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever sells or has in possession for sale any goods with a counterfeit property mark affixed, shall, unless he proves that he had no reason to suspect the genuineness of the mark and gave all information about the source, be punished with imprisonment up to one year, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Selling Counterfeit Goods

Essential elements:

(i) Sells or has in possession for sale goods with counterfeit property mark

counterfeit goods

selling counterfeit

property mark

burden of proof

X sells fake branded watches. X is punishable with up to 1 year imprisonment unless he proves he didn't know they were fake and told police who sold them to him.

BNS SECTION 350 - MAKING A FALSE MARK UPON ANY RECEPTACLE CONTAINING GOODS
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever makes any false mark upon any case, package or other receptacle containing goods, calculated to cause any person to believe that it contains goods which it does not contain, or that the goods are of different nature or quality, shall, unless he proves that he acted without intent to defraud, be punished with imprisonment up to three years, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: False Mark on Receptacle

Essential elements:

false mark

BNS SECTION 351 - CRIMINAL INTIMIDATION
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever threatens another with any injury to his person, reputation or property, with intent to cause alarm to that person, or to cause that person to do any act which he is not legally bound to do, commits criminal intimidation. Punishment is imprisonment up to two years, or fine, or both. If threat is to cause death or grievous hurt, or to cause destruction of property by fire, or to impute unchastity to a woman, imprisonment up to seven years, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Criminal Intimidation

Essential elements:

(i) Threatens another with injury to person, reputation, or property

(ii) Intent to cause alarm OR to cause that person to do an act he is not legally bound to do

Standard criminal intimidation: Imprisonment up to 2 years OR fine OR both

Aggravated threats (death/grievous hurt/destruction by fire/imputing unchastity to woman): Imprisonment up to 7 years OR fine OR both

KEYWORDS:

criminal intimidation

threat

injury

alarm

aggravated threat

unchastity

ILLUSTRATION:
X threatens to break Y's windows unless Y pays him ₹5,000. X is punishable with up to 2 years imprisonment. X threatens to kill Y unless Y signs over his property. X is punishable with up to 7 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 352 - INTENTIONAL INSULT WITH INTENT TO PROVOKE BREACH OF PEACE
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever intentionally insults in any manner, and thereby gives provocation to any person, intending or knowing it to be likely that such provocation will cause him to break the public peace, shall be punished with imprisonment up to two years, or fine, or both.

PROCEDURE: Intentional Insult

Essential elements:

(i) Intentionally insults in any manner

(ii) Thereby gives provocation

(iii) Intends or knows it is likely to cause the person to break public peace

intentional insult

provocation

breach of peace

public peace

BNS SECTION 353 - STATEMENTS CONDUCING TO PUBLIC MISCHIEF
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever makes, publishes or circulates any statement, false information, rumour or report with intent to cause any officer to mutiny, or to cause fear or alarm to the public, or to incite any class of persons to commit an offence, shall be punished with imprisonment up to three years, or fine, or both. If committed in place of worship, imprisonment up to five years and fine.

PROCEDURE: Public Mischief

Essential elements:

(i) Makes, publishes, or circulates statement, false information, rumour, or report

public mischief

false information

rumour

incite

place of worship

X spreads a false rumour that a particular community is planning to attack another, causing widespread fear. X is punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment. If X does this inside a temple, up to 5 years imprisonment.

BNS SECTION 354 - ACT CAUSED BY INDUCING PERSON TO BELIEVE HE WILL BE RENDERED AN OBJECT OF DIVINE DISPLEASURE
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever voluntarily causes or attempts to cause any person to do anything which that person is not legally bound to do, by inducing that person to believe that he will become an object of Divine displeasure if he does not do that thing, shall be punished with imprisonment up to one year, or fine, or both.

BNS SECTION 355 - MISCONDUCT IN PUBLIC BY A DRUNKEN PERSON

Whoever, in a state of intoxication, appears in any public place and conducts himself in such a manner as to cause annoyance to any person, shall be punished with simple imprisonment up to twenty-four hours, or fine up to one thousand rupees, or both, or with community service.

BNS SECTION 356 - DEFAMATION
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever, by words either spoken or intended to be read, or by signs or by visible representations, makes or publishes any imputation concerning any person intending to harm the reputation of such person, is said to defame that person. Exceptions: truth for public good, opinion on public servant's conduct, opinion on any public question, substantially true report of court proceedings, opinion on merits of any case, opinion on merits of any performance, censure by person having authority over another, good faith accusation, good faith caution. Punishment is simple imprisonment up to two years, or fine, or both, or with community service.

PROCEDURE: Defamation

Exceptions (not defamation):

Truth for public good

Opinion on public servant's conduct

Opinion on any public question

Substantially true report of court proceedings

Opinion on merits of any case

Opinion on merits of any performance

Censure by person having authority over another

Good faith accusation

Good faith caution

Punishment: Simple imprisonment up to 2 years OR fine OR both OR community service

KEYWORDS:

defamation

imputation

harm reputation

truth for public good

fair comment

community service

ILLUSTRATION:

BNS SECTION 357 - BREACH OF CONTRACT TO ATTEND ON AND SUPPLY WANTS OF HELPLESS PERSON
CORE CONTENT:
Whoever, being bound by a lawful contract to attend on or to supply the wants of any person who by reason of youth, unsoundness of mind, disease or bodily weakness is helpless or incapable of providing for his own safety, voluntarily omits so to do, shall be punished with imprisonment up to three months, or fine up to five thousand rupees, or both.

BNS SECTION 358 - REPEAL AND SAVINGS
CORE CONTENT:
The Indian Penal Code (45 of 1860) is hereby repealed. The repeal does not affect the previous operation of the Code or anything duly done thereunder, or any right, privilege, obligation or liability acquired, accrued or incurred under the Code, or any penalty or punishment incurred in respect of any offences committed against the Code, or any investigation or remedy in respect of such penalty or punishment. Any proceeding may be instituted, continued or enforced as if the Code had not been repealed.

PROCEDURE: Repeal and Savings

IPC 1860 is repealed

Savings (things that continue unaffected):

(i) Previous operation of IPC

(ii) Anything duly done under IPC

(iii) Any right, privilege, obligation, or liability acquired, accrued, or incurred under IPC

(iv) Any penalty or punishment incurred for offences against IPC

(v) Any investigation or remedy in respect of such penalty or punishment

Proceedings may be instituted, continued, or enforced as if IPC had not been repealed

KEYWORDS:

repeal

Indian Penal Code

savings

pending proceedings

continuing effect

ILLUSTRATION:
X committed theft in 2022 under the IPC. The trial is ongoing when BNS comes into force. The case will continue to be tried under the IPC as if it had not been repealed. Any rights X had under the IPC remain protected.
